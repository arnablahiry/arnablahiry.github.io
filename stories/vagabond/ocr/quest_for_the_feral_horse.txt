Quest for the feral horse

Cooking Maggi is quite dangerous and sometimes proves to be
life threatening. Since the journey was long and arduous, to say
the least, provisions needed to be carried. In some places, the
only provisions procurable, cook able and eatable with any
degree of safety to the internal organs are still eggs and the
ubiquitous Maggi. The search for the wild feral horse had to be
undergone in a small wooden dinghy, floating through rivers and
rivulets across dense forests, open grasslands and meadows, over
huge stretches of blistering sand and quaint Mishing villages in
Dibru Saikhowa National Park in the district of Tinsukia, Assam.
Though the month was November, the mid days were blistering

but the mornings were cool and the nights, cold.

Thad landed up on the banks of Dibru river in Dibru Saikhowa
not knowing what to expect on a cold November morning,
lugging my camera and lens and precious little else. I knew about
the existence of the only population of feral horse in Asia in the
recesses of the Dibru Saikhowa National Park and getting their
images was priority number one. Little did I know that in that
process I would land up in the house of an outlawed outfit’s
leader who was under surveillance, to see Hoolock Gibbons, be
chased by an irate wild bull buffalo while following a pair of Pied
Hornbills, would almost meet my maker when trapped in a
quagmire, would spend a scorching afternoon perched yards
away from a herd of wild buffaloes on a wobbly dinghy and see

[Page 47]
them eat, sleep, frolic and mate and even get myself blown up
and drowned while trying to cook Maggi.

Let me tell the story of the feral horse, cooking maggi and

quagmire... well, not a story but the sequence of real incidents.

Two of my friends, both Punjabis and built on generous lines in
the tradition of the Land of Five rivers who cannot be named
because they had bunked office to join me, were my companions
along with my local guide and one boatman who supposedly

knew the hideout of the feral horses.

When the eastern horizon started to get light and the jagged
peaks of the Himalayas of NEFA (Arunachal Pradesh) just
started getting visible in the horizon, a small dinghy with a rather
hoarse diesel engine huffed and puffed to the ghat to start the
journey. It was a nice little boat with a few small holes at certain
points which did not sink her but gave her a character. It had a
nice bit of plastic cover covering the midships. However, the
plastic covering was a deterrent to unhindered photography
from the boat. The problem was solved to a certain extent by
throwing a narrow plank of roughly hewn wood across the bow.
Now I had an unhindered access for my lens but the effects

showed in my hindquarters when we returned.

The swift flowing river silently glided by till we made a turn into
one of the channels. This channel was liberally studded with dead
stumps of trees and jutting rocks, creating swiftly flowing rapids.
Our boatman skilfully guided the boat as the sky rapidly

56

[Page 48]
lightened. A small village retreated swiftly and we were sailing
inside a forest. Flocks of parakeets flew purposefully towards the
village to feed on the ripening vegetable patches. Yellow footed
green pigeons thronged the tree tops and Lesser Cormorants had
already started their morning fishing. Suddenly over the chatter
of birds and the gurgle of the river there rose a tremendous
hooting noise as if a bunch of rowdy school kids had been let
loose in the local sweet shop without the owner present. Hoolock
Gibbons!!! They hooted, taunted, hollered, screeched and the
whole place throbbed with their racket.

Soon the Hoolock Gibbons were left behind and the river wove
in and out through the jungle. The tree stumps receded and the
river became slightly placid in parts but the underlying current
was unmistakable since the debris in the form of branches and
leaves were carried down at an alarming rate of knots. I kept
myself perched on the piece of wood taking pot shots at Pied
Kingfishers, Crested Serpent Eagles and one Greater Cormorant
with no success. We had a long way to go and slowing down for
our feathered friends was not an option. That did not stop me
from getting ecstatic and exclaim at regular intervals... ‘ohhhhh
a Stork Billed Kingfisher’... ‘ahhhh a Black Headed Gull till a
helicopter from one of the forward airbases flew in and one of
my friends from the Land of the Five Rivers sat back comfortably
on my rucksack and smugly uttered, ‘wowwwww a Rotating
Headed Metal Bird’. That effectively stopped my ornithological

expressions.

Around mid-morning we reached a forest guard camp. We
disembarked to make enquiries about animal movements from
the guards. Apparently, a small herd of elephants and a few wild
buffaloes were roaming the vicinity of the camp. A look around
from the top of the tower yielded nothing. By the time we came
back to our boat, hunger was gnawing and it was time for
breakfast.

Maggi and eggs.

The boat was cast off and we sailed on. The packets of Maggi and
a crate of eggs were brought out. A can of kerosene appeared and
finally out came what seemed like a stove. It looked like a stove
and probably was one of the earliest versions invented by man.
Our guide, who doubled up as the cook, bent down, placed the
stove at the bottom, poured in the kerosene and started
pumping. All of us except the boatman looked on, fascinated.
When we were nearing a sand bar, our guide decided that the
stove has been adequately pumped up and picked up a match
stick and lighted it.

All I saw was a small ball of flame and a whooooosh sound. The
boatman let go of the tiller and we headed straight for the sand
bar. My Punjabi friends decided not to wait till landfall and
promptly jumped overboard. They did not know swimming but
fortunately we were almost at the sand bar and the depth was not
much but the splash my friends created woke up the jungle. A
whole flock of Fruit Bats took to the air from a tree overhanging

the river. A Great Egret floating lazily up the river in front of us

58

[Page 50]
gave a honk of alarm and rose straight up towards the sky. A
flock of Little Cormorants dived for safety. A Little Heron who
was trying to disguise itself quite successfully as a branch of a
tree, shot across the river at supersonic speed. I, being an ex-
mariner, could not bring myself to desert my ship because of my
years of shipboard training but the boatman cut off the engine
and jumped. The boat slowed down because of the current of the
river and beached with a hiss of crushing pebbles. The fire was
burning in the bottom of the boat. All through this my guide sat
calmly on the side sill of the boat and watched. Within minutes
the fire burnt off the oil that was floating at the bottom of the
boat. Miraculously, the kerosene inside the stove did not catch
fire. It was almost an hour before we finished cooking our
breakfast on the sand bar since all of us vehemently opposed the
idea of our guide who still wanted to cook the meal while

underway.

We set sail again with our belies full though a little shaken. A
long stretch of dense forest for next two hours abruptly opened
upon a tribal village nestled serenely on the banks of the river.
Hordes of buffaloes were grazing or were being milked. Naked
children ran along the bank crying in delight at our lenses. Small
dugout boats were setting out for the long and tortuous journey
to the village from where we had started to deliver a can or two
of milk. Ladies were repairing fishing nets. The hustle and bustle
of the village deep inside the forest contrasted beautifully with
the serene surroundings of the meandering river, the

surrounding forest and the peaks of the Eastern Himalayas.

We journeyed on but at one point our boat ran aground though
the river looked wide. The boatman asked us to disembark to
lighten the boat and walk some distance till the river was deep
enough. One of my friends flatly refused. He was already badly
shaken with the explosion and his dip in the river and did not
want a stroll in the forest. He was right. My other friend, our
guide and myself started the walk and it was strenuous to say the
least. The river had created sand banks and each step in the mid-
day sun was an effort. However, my generously built Punjabi
friend relished the stroll and charged across the vast sand banks
with stunted trees growing here and there till we came to a point
where a vast herd of domestic buffaloes from the village were
crossing too. My friend and the herd eyed each other
suspiciously and finally the herd crossed keeping an eye cocked
on my friend lest he made funny business. We walked almost five
kilometres till our boat found enough water to navigate and we

sailed on.

After scaring a flock of Great Cormorants on a sand bar we
finally made landfall at an island where our feral horses were
supposed to be residing as per reports of our guide and boatman.

Here it is pertinent to give a short note on these horses.

During World War II the Japanese surrendered after retreating.
The allied soldiers were using horses to transport their men and
material across the inhospitable terrain. When the war ended, the
soldiers returned home but left the horses to fend for themselves.

They roamed these forests with the tiger, elephant and wild

60

[Page 52]
buffalo and slowly the genes of their forefathers took over. They
became the only population of feral horses in Asia.

They were supposed to be nearby but a thorough sweep with our
binoculars did not show any. We pushed on inside the island.
The island was crisscrossed with rivers and semi dry river beds
and the ground was sandy and sometimes muddy. After futilely
plodding for quite some distance, our guide suggested that we
split in two groups and search in a circle. My group had hardly
gone fifty metres when frantic whistles broke out from the other
group. A mad rush across the sands and dodging in and out of
the Casuarina trees and we came out in the open on the other

side.

In front lay a large expanse of semi dry river bed. The sun was
high up and miniature sand storms swirled and danced across
the damp earth and sandy soil. On the other side of the river bed
stretched the stunted Casuarina trees, green, swaying in the hot

breeze. The massive Himalayas sat in grand solitude....... and in

the green haze of the Casuarinas, like a few flickers of flames,
roamed the feral horses, their reddish coat gleaming in the sun.
Their manes which had not been cared for by the hand of man
were caressed by the breeze and the wild muscles rippled under

the flaming fur. My tryst was complete.

After we had our fill of shots, I sat close to them without
arousing suspicion, marvelling at the beauty and timelessness of
man’s fascination with wild horses. Finally, they grew suspicious

and galloped away into the horizon in a thunder of hooves. It

[Page 53]
was time to turn back. This is where I had the final icing on the
cake so to speak.

We were walking back in a single file. Still drunk with my close
encounter with the horses, I had lagged behind somewhat from
the rest of the file. My camera felt heavy and sweat poured in
profusion. My throat was parched and the swirling dust did not
improve things. The sand under the feet felt heavier with every
stride. That is why an even and solid looking patch of tightly
packed mud looked inviting enough for me to try and take a
shortcut. I confidently stepped on the patch and in a blink of an
eye sank waist deep in oozing mud. I was not unduly perturbed
but held my camera high to avoid getting it damaged and planted
my other foot to balance myself and get out of the quagmire.
This foot too went down and panic set in. I was sinking in the
mud fast and in panic I lost my voice. Just when I thought all was
lost and thoughts of my mortal remains remaining hidden in a
far corner of North East India started flashing through in my
brain my guide felt something wrong, and returned back. When I
was extricated and sat on a firm piece of land, I looked and
shivered at the black oozing mud which had by now replaced the
solid looking patch because of my exertions and suddenly felt

cold and shivered.

Death and exhilaration were my companions, going hand in

hand on my quest for the feral horse. The experience was

humbling. The majestic blue mountains and the vast river plains,

the tribal Mishings ferrying their milk in dugouts and the logs

being carried down the deep blue river, the smell of the damp hot
