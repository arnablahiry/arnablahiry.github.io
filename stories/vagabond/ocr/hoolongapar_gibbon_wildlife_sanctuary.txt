I never drink when in field especially in elephant territory. This
night was an exception. After a day’s successful hunt with the
lens we were offered Rice Beer. Rice Beer is a local brew
throughout Assam and though not something to write home
about, this was different. This particular strain of paddy from
which this brew was made was grown in a small patch of land
adjacent to Diganta’s home. This brew was mixed with more
than forty-two types of various herbs handpicked from the forest
and the resultant elixir just numbed the senses (not literally since
it has to be consumed in quite some quantity to achieve the state
of nirvana). Diganta’s cook conjured up a dish of dry pork with

garlic and a dash of chillies.

It was the month of October and the rains had not totally
retreated. Night had fallen. Sougata and I reclined comfortably in
the sofas in the sitting room of our home stay. The vast paddy
fields starting from beneath our window was dark. Darkness
stretched to the horizon. A late evening shower had stopped by
then. Frogs croaked in unison urging on their mates while water
droplets from the surrounding trees on the roof completed the
sound effects. The monsoon gods threw flashes across the cloud
laden sky from time to time to liven up things as the distant
bamboo fronds got illuminated against the sky. Thunder
rumbled like the ocean breaking the waves on a boulder strewn
seafront.

We had hardly taken a couple of sips when all of a sudden there
were excited voices outside the door. A group of youth rushed in
and informed Diganta that a herd of elephants were raiding the
paddy fields nearby. We picked up our torches and followed
them into the night.

Soon we left the road and entered a narrow unpaved pathway
that led through village huts and small patches of paddy and
vegetables. The village did not have electricity. Light from the oil
lamps in the huts spilled onto the path. Small rivulets gurgled
down the sides, where children, drenched in water and mud in
equal measure, caught small fish with strips of cloth. We hurried
down the path and soon came upon a large field. Torches made
from sticks wrapped in kerosene-soaked cloths were stuck in the
branches of trees surrounding the field throwing a ghostly
flickering light all around. The jungle surrounded the field on
three sides and a paddy field was on the fourth. Shadows were
darting around in the half-darkness shouting and brandishing

sticks. A couple of crackers went off somewhere.

Since we were trying to do a video shoot of the place, Sougata
stood me beside a torch tied to a tree for a descriptive dialogue of
the goings on. With the pandemonium going on all around, my
tongue got stuck to my throat and I made a thorough hash of it.
When we reached the paddy field, the elephants had departed
leaving behind patches of destroyed crops and some dung. Forest
Department jeeps streamed in but the jumbos had already shown

a clean pair of heels by that time.

Hoolongapar Gibbon Wildlife Sanctuary takes its name from the
Hoolong tree and is one of the smallest sanctuaries covering an
area of about 21 sq. km. Within these narrow confines reside
some of the most astonishing fauna of the sub-continent. It is not
the elephants or leopards that attracted me to this evergreen
forest. This sanctuary is home to Western Hoolock Gibbons,
Capped Langurs, Stump-tailed macaques. Pig-tailed macaques,
Assam macaques, Bengal Slow Loris and Tokay Geckos in

adequate numbers.

This is one of the very few sanctuaries where there are no
vehicles. Even Bharatpur has rickshaws and horse drawn carts
but here you have to walk. Walking in a dense evergreen forest
with an armed guard escort has a charm which is seldom enjoyed
when you are going after a tiger or rhino or elephant in a Gypsy.
You are dwarfed by the massive trees on both sides of the
pathways sometimes blotting out the sun. There is always the
chance of meeting elephants but the armed guard keeps you safe.
If you have the stomach for it, you can go for a night walk in the
outskirts of the forest when the chances of seeing a Bengal Slow
Loris is highest though we did not meet any even after repeated
tries. What we met were the elusive and highly endangered
Tokay Gecko. However, the heart-warming sight was a man-
made iron structure over the railway line which runs through the

jungle.

When the railway line was planned and built, nobody spared a

thought about the denizens of the forest. Now, Gibbons are

primarily arboreal and are not able to walk on the ground let

[Page 159]
alone traversing railway lines. They are immensely acrobatic in
the forest canopy but depends solely on the intertwining
branches of the trees for moving from one place to another. The
railway line divided not only the forest but the Gibbon families as
well. They could not cross from one side to the other. This led to
inbreeding and the resultant deformities. Someone somewhere

came to realise this.

This realisation led to the construction of an iron arch
constructed across the railway line. When we visited, the arch
was being given the finishing touches. Hopefully the Gibbons
across the LOC would realize the potential of this arch and start
using the arch to traverse the full length of the jungle using this
arch and the families that were separated by a man-made
construction would find another man-made construction to
reunite. A forest North Korea finally has a chance to merge with

a forest South Korea.

And then finally we went for our last walk in the jungle. We had
walked the full length of the forest trail. The Gibbons had made
their appearance, Capped Langurs had lazed on the branches, a
Malayan Giant Squirrels had nibbled at ripe figs above us and
Pig-tailed Macaques had done their frolicking on the branches
above us. The weather was a bit sultry but the sweat felt good
from the satisfaction of watching some of the most elusive
animals on the planet. Bird life was prolific with a distant sight of
the elusive Red-headed Trogon. Photography was difficult
because of the high and dense canopy but when you sight White-

browed Piculets, Dollarbirds, Emerald Cuckoos in their natural

The forest was alive with the chatter of birds returning to roost.
Light was fading fast. The trees stood motionless crowding us
from all around in the stillness. Frangipani had started blooming
with the onset of the night and the sweet heady smell permeated
the air. Some trees had already started shedding their leaves and
they fell to the forest floor with loud plops. Parakeets streaked
across the darkening sky like little green arrows. Jackals had
started their haunting calls from the depths of the interspersed
glades. Serenity pervaded everything when suddenly our guide
stopped and pointed to a tree across a small water body towards
a jamun tree. Looking carefully, we saw a group of eight Stump-
tailed Macaques perched on the branches. The Stump-tailed and
Assam Macaques had eluded us till then.

As we watched them, the alpha male climbed down to a lower
branch next to a female and sat down regally reclining on the
main trunk. Soon the lady started flirting around. The male set
an uninterested face but kept giving furtive glances. Then he
could stand the advances no longer and the process started. The
light was too low and they kept a branch of the tree in between
them and me for modesty’s sake. As we watched on, suddenly a
bush just below them came alive and a spotted paw shot up. By
some miracle, the lovers jumped at once and the paw missed by a
hair’s breadth. The rest of the group had started being vocal but I
thought they had been a bit lax in their duties as watchmen. I
mean, when the leader is busy with important duties, it is the

duty of the foot soldiers to watch his back but they had failed
miserably in their duties. The leopard did not follow up in its
attack.... maybe it was not hungry enough or may be the
monkeys had escaped to narrow branches which would not

support its weight.

Gibbon Wildlife Sanctuary is not for the tiger specific visitor. It is
for people who love offbeat destinations and cherish the
biodiversity of the planet. It is for the exquisite species, the feel of
a walk in an evergreen forest, the sound of dew drops falling on
the leaves at night while you look for Slow Loris, savour a local
brew in the evening which could give Drambuie a run for its
money, where you can fill your senses to the brim with the
hooting call of the Hoolock Gibbons in the morning, where you
can see man trying to somewhat reverse the process of
devastation he has created by constructing a pathway for the

Gibbons to cross forests.
