Miracle of migration

God found himself at his wits end. He placed all the Godwits
here. The boat in which the rides are conducted have to, literally,
navigate through a thicket of legs of the Black Winged Stilts. By
the time you finish your stint with the Stilts you will not want to
see another Stilt in your life. The number of Sandpipers is so
mind boggling that I thought I had downed a bottle of Bagpiper
before the start of the day and hence, was hallucinating. The
phenomenon called Mangalajodi is easy to believe if you have
not seen the place but very hard to believe once you have

witnessed the spectacle.

On the right-hand side of the embankment where the trail ends
and the boat ride starts is a patch of grass which looks like a
chicken hatchery. The swampy patch of ground is overrun with
Purple Swamphens. A few Open Billed Storks and Glossy Ibises
accompanied by an army of Black Winged Stilts and Jack Snipes
make an attempt from time to time to capture the mud patch but
are soon summarily side-lined by the purple army. I sat by the
side of the embankment to try to capture the action when a
Bluethroat interrupted my endeavour by almost popping into my
lens. T gave him my due respect by taking a few snaps and then
moved farther away and concentrated on the war of the mud
patch. I had hardly looked into my view finder when there he
was, the Bluethroat, staring at me with innocent eyes and a
pained look as if I had not paid enough attention to his charms. I

felt ashamed and clicked a few more before moving away to what

Disgustedly I picked up my lens without paying heed to the
seductive call of the Bluethroat and walked down the trail
watching the Ruddy Breasted Crakes foraging among the reed
beds. Handsome chaps with blood shot eyes as if they had taken
a few puffs of Ganja. A flock of Ibises floated lazily in the
morning breeze over the vast wetlands and landed as softly as
down on a patch of open water. Two Brahminy Shelducks circled
overhead like two floating flames looking for a suitable patch of

unoccupied property.

They called to each other in that questioning sort of way as if
discussing the merits of the property on display. A flock of
Godwits some thousands strong took to the air at a distance as an
eagle flew high above. As the sun climbed overhead, the horizon
simmered in lazy heat. The red dust of the soil of the
embankment mixed with the smell of the decaying vegetation

and sweat trickled down my shirt making it pleasantly damp.

A small rusty striped body flew low over the red earth and landed
ona bush nearby. I have been trying to capture the Common,
Hoopoe for quite some time but without success and here was
one sitting hardly 20 feet away and preening away without a care
in the world. Click click went my D-90. Nice... let me see if I can
manage to get a few feet closer. ‘I'he distance shortened to about

15 feet as I clicked merrily away. As far as my model was

join the party. We approached to almost 6 feet of the bird and I

However, Avishek wanted me to take a picture of him and the
bird with him patting the Hoopoe's crest, for his Facebook
profile. With this singular aim in mind he boldly strode up to the
astonished bird who looked at him as if affronted by his audacity.
When Avishek had reached his to-be profile buddy, the Hoopoe
could not take this any longer and with an admonishing cry took

to its wings.

Mangalajodi is a fisherman's village which was a trapper’s
heaven. The transformation from poacher to conservationist has
seen spectacular results. However, the first signs of tourism
generated revenue are slowly becoming evident. A school here, a
few electricity posts there, a pukka road running through...

Plenty needs to be done but a starting has been made.

In a small corner of this world here in the marshes and swamps
on the northern fringes of Chilika Lake in Orissa, among the
brackish water, the feathered denizens of this planet have found a

home without the persecution of civilization... till now.
