The last of the Dooars

A Sigma 150-500 with a Nikon D90 body is not exactly a
lightweight contraption to carry on elephant back, especially if
there is nothing to hold on to except for a jute sack thrown on
the behemoth's back. However, I decided to take it along when
going on an elephant ride inside the jungle so that a rhino or
elephant won't escape my lens even at a distance. Half an hour
into the elephant ride and my elephant almost tripped over a pair
of rhinos inside a dense undergrowth. At a range of about 15 feet,
neither the rhinos nor my elephant was prepared to budge and
there was a stalemate. At this critical juncture, my big lens flatly
refused to focus on such a huge subject at so short a distance and
all I came out with were a few shots of grey blur which had to be
identified as rhinos and not pieces of boulder. In despair I almost

threw away my gear.

Next day better sense prevailed and I carried with me my 18-105
lens. This time one of my fellow travellers did not find it
comfortable riding piggyback on an elephant with no railings to
hold his bulk and demanded he be taken back after the elephant
ride had started. By the time elephant ride was resumed, the sun
had begun to tilt noticeably. After watching great swathes of
grassland and dense forest without visible sign of wildlife
(though there was plenty of audible signs) we turned back. The
sun was down and gloom was settling in with heavy clouds
gathering on the slopes of Bhutan Mountains as the glens

reverberated with the plaintive cries of the peacocks. As we
approached the Murti river, the grass in front of us parted to
reveal a full herd of about forty Gaurs. My camera focused and
this time I came up with a load of brown blobs which had to be
identified in person as Gaurs. In that gathering gloom there was
no chance of getting a single decent photograph of the herd at a
distance of about 30 feet. I still do not know how I managed to

restrain myself from throwing down my gear.

I was not particularly asleep since the rat-infested wooden
cottage with a thatched roof built on a single concrete pillar, did
not have electricity. North Bengal in the month of September can

be a little sultry.

My son and daughter were fast asleep when a great commotion
broke outside my cottage with somebody shouting and the sound
of a heavy body rushing. My wife being next to the window, got
up and looked out of the window and excitedly whispered to me
"elephant". I rushed to the window and peered out. The pitch
darkness outside was pierced by a wildly flailing torchlight.
"Where is the elephant?" My wife took my head in her hands and
turned it right in front of our cottage and there below me was

one of the most astonishing sights I had ever witnessed.

A female elephant, with its trunk outstretched in its front like a
scimitar and the tail outstretched behind her like a flag, was
running madly towards the forest from the paddy field in front of
us and was being pursued by an old man with a small torch,

hollering for all he was worth. The man chased the scared
