The sub-adult elephant was wild. I was convinced about it by the
way it grazed next to the forest camp. It did not have the tell-tale
mark of the captive elephants’ shackles around the ankles. Vinit
and Anirudh were from Central India and hence were not that
familiar with elephants. Sougata however, was dead sure that it
belonged to the forest department and was tame. Hence, to prove
his point he made a peculiar chuck chucking sound at the
elephant probably expecting it to say hi to him with a wave of its
trunk. Instead there was an ear-splitting triaaaaaaaank and the
next moment the hulk was thundering down the road towards

us.

The driver put the engine of the Gypsy in reverse and we put a
decent distance between us and the pachyderm. Having been
foiled in its efforts to get a closer look at us, the young fellow
glared at us standing in the middle of the road, then reluctantly
left the path and slowly... very slowly strolled towards a grassy
patch inside the forest.

This gave us the chance and our driver revved up the engine and
sped down the road, past the point where the charge took place.
We were merrily careering on when a gust of wind caught Vinit’s
beloved jungle cap and tossed it off his head onto the road. A
stalemate ensued.... Vinit being too fond of his cap, which had

stayed with him for years in his tiger terrain, refused to budge

Finally, Vinit got down with the guard.... walked down the road,

A little distance down the same road and we saw two gentlemen
walking down the road with gunny bags full of groceries and
stores. They were forest guards; they were not even armed. One
carried a hefty pumpkin but as a matter of protection from
charging elephants, it hardly counted.

“What will they do if the elephant decides to charge when they
are walking on the road?”, Vinit asked the guard.

“Saab if they meet the elephant on the road, they will not need to
do anything... whatever needs to be done will be done by the
elephant”, replied the guard laconically. We drove back
pondering in silence. These guards are unsung but their valour is
probably no less than the bravest of the brave our country has

produced.

That evening we were driving back through a dark and damp

buffalos looked at us curiously wallowing in the swampy

ground... and then the tyre went flat.

By the time the tyre was replaced it was dark and some leeches

The icing on the cake took place on our way back from Manas.
We were travelling towards Alipurduar from where Sougata and
I were to catch our train back to Calcutta while Vinit and
Anirudh would be driving on their way to Nagpur. We were
having tea in a shop outside the forest where we started chatting
with an old gentleman who knew Manas well. We were saddened

that we did not find the Golden Langurs and told him so.

“But they are there... but not in the forest. There is a small village
a long way down where the villagers have protected a troop of
them around their village. You can go and see if they are still
around”, said the old man and gave us the directions.

Following his directions, we landed at the village hoping against
hope to have a glimpse of the elusive Langurs. The villagers
received us quite enthusiastically and soon we were threading
our way through a dense bamboo grove squinting our eyes
towards the canopy of the bamboo fronds. Soon something
bright and golden peeped at us from the thicket. We had our first
sighting of the Golden Langur.

Mad with joy we tried to get a better look when the whole troop
started moving across the bamboo grove and we followed at a

mad pace. They reached a tree line and there they rested.

What an amazing sight.... about fifteen of them spread out across
the tree tops and simply lazed there. The sun set their golden furs
on fire. Mothers caressed their babies. Males lazily plucked some
ripe figs and like monarchs, crossed their legs across the

Manas might not have high profile leaders from across the world
come visiting. Manas does not have proper facilities for tourism
like some of the major Tiger Reserves and National parks. The
forest staff is inadequate to keep vigil across the forest but still
they carry out their jobs with dedication... or at least most of
them do. Manas does not have lines of Gypsies lining the
entrance with hordes of tourists spilling from every nook and
cranny. Manas only has pristine beauty.... many places
untouched and unspoilt by humans. Manas looks what this earth
was supposed to look and probably did from a time long
forgotten in our modern world. Pygmy Hog, Bengal Florican,
Hispid Hare.... Manas is one of their last homes on this planet

before they vanish. It is worth saving.
