The story of Dream the Leopard Cub

The little fur ball climbed up my chest and snatched away my

heart.

My friend Gautam got a call from the forest department that a
leopard cub had been found in the tea gardens off Margherita,
Upper Assam and needed to be released and since he worked
with the wildlife in Kaziranga, his advice and presence would be
of help. The cub was found by one of the locals inside the garden
and picked up and handed over to the forest dept. Since the
Ranger was out of station nobody knew what to do and already 3
days had passed and we were losing time with every passing day.
As a species, we humans are so obnoxious that even a leopard
mother might not accept back her baby if there is a detectable
human imprint and here the baby was being hand reared by the

servants. We decided to try our luck that evening.

The plan was to take the cub to the location where she was
found, try look for pug marks or some other indications of the
mother’s presence and leave her there and watch from a safe
distance inside our Gypsy all the night if required. We hoped
that the cries of the cub would attract the mother and hopefully
they would reunite. In case the cub was rejected we would bring

her back. We were yet to see the cub.

Gautam, Mr. Deka the forest guard and myself drove down to
Margherita town that afternoon and alighted at the ranger’s
office. We thought that the cub would be housed in a cage away
from prying eyes. Instead a servant boy came up holding a
cardboard box and from it peered a soulful pair of blue eyes with
a face that might have melted the heart of Hannibal and could
only be called - DREAM. The cub was hardly 2 months old and
she clambered up my arms purring like a tabby. She was not even
weaned and was being fed with milk from a bottle. There was no

way we could abandon her in the wild without her mother.

We set out immediately accompanied by 2 men of the forest
department who knew the location. When we reached the place,
it presented still further problems. The tea garden was flanked on
2 sides by dense jungle and the furrows were so deep that if we
lost the cub it would be impossible to get her back in the
gathering gloom. Then it started drizzling.

The cub was put down on the ground and immediately she
started calling and tried to disappear inside the tea plants. We
collected her and went down the middle of the plantation. Ever
tried to release a leopard cub in a tea garden in the midst of a tea
garden flanked by jungles in a drizzle wearing bathroom slippers
and carrying a DSLR where the chances of stumbling upon an

angry mother leopard are quite high? Do not try it.

At one point we got the scent of the leopard quite strongly but by
that time the dusk was gathering and the risk was too high. Still

we set the cub down but it immediately vanished inside the tea

plants and was found more by luck than by tracking skills. We
realized that this was almost a futile effort since we could not get
the gypsy so far inside and leaving the cub to its fate would most
likely get it killed either by other leopards or by the elephants
which pass by regularly or even by snake bite. Standing guard on
the ground the whole night would put all of us at high risk.

We searched for a safer location to set Dream down but soon it
was dark and there was not a spot in sight where she would be
out of harm’s way. The search was abandoned and we carried her
back to the Ranger’s house. From that night the floods of Assam
set in and after that there was no point in looking for the mother.

Before leaving I paid one more visit just to hold Dream in my
arms and promise her that her story will be told even if nobody
listens. Those trusting eyes will probably not see her mother
again. The Forest Department did not know what to do with her.
The zoos apparently were not willing take her in for lack of
space. There was no plan of action in place in the state where
they are found in one of the highest numbers. They do not even
have a cage for such a contingency and none of the famous NGO
s have thought about doing something about it. There is no
awareness campaign to educate people living around the forests
that leopards or other wildlife are not to be touched let alone
picked up unless wounded in which case the department is to be
notified. Setting dream free would have been akin to murdering
her. She was nobody’s child. Forest Department was caring for
her at that moment but what was the long-term solution nobody
knew. Dream did not belong to a place where people would hold

candle light vigils and put up posters for her survival. I too could
not do anything for her except for telling her story and I am

Those eyes and that purring call will haunt me forever in my
dreams when my Dream calls out and find that her calls have no
ears to listen to. In a far-flung corner of India in a half-forgotten
place a little furball was searching for her mother. I only hoped
my Dream would live.... she would be in my heart till it stops
beating. After I left and came back home, Gautam called me to
let me know that Guwahati zoo had finally accepted her. She
would live her life behind bars.... alone.... looking at gawking
visitors who would poke fun at her from the safety beyond the
cage but nobody will know her story. At least dream will live and

that was more than I could hope for when I left her.

...and it all started with a phone call from Gautam while I was
fast asleep one Sunday afternoon - “come fast.... the floods have
started in Kaziranga”. That is what I had longed to see for a long
time. My association with Gautam and Kaziranga goes back for
quite a few years but I was yet to see Kazi during the floods.
Every time the floods started, I was held up due to some reason
or the other but this time there was no stopping me. I boarded
the Kamrup Express the next day and headed for Furkating, the
nearest railhead for Kazi.

When J alighted at one o’clock at night Gautam and my old
trusted driver Bubul were waiting for me. ‘lhe pleasantries over

Gautam suggested that we travel up and down the National

Highway bordering Kaziranga and that we did. From one to four
we travelled up and down the highway and spotted a herd of
Wild Buffalo along with the usual Hog Deer and spent the next

few hours at Gautam’s house snoring away.

That morning we travelled to Gautam’s camp and this time I did
not get to experience my usual roller coaster ride over the fallow
fields bordering the Dhanseri river... because there were no
fields... everything was under water and we travelled by boat over

the same place where I usually travel by Gypsy.

Since Kaziranga was closed, I usually spent my days with the
camp workers, collecting driftwood on the Brahmaputra and that
is when we met a Softshell turtle on one of the river islands
which was the size of a medium sized dining table. Rambabu was
carrying almost half a dead tree on his incredibly strong
shoulders in waist deep mud back to the boat when the turtle
showed itself by moving. It was truly a magnificent species but
had an injury mark near the neck which needed closer inspection
for which we needed to turn it on its back. Easier said than done.
As the workers tried to close in, the turtle struck at incredible
speed and those open jaws could do a lot of damage. Evading
those jaws in the waist deep slimy mud with camera in hand was
not the easiest of jobs but the rest of the party were having a
tougher time trying to turn it over. The huge lady tried to haul
herself over the mud and disappear into the river. Rambabu, in a

final desperate attempt, got on her shell and I had the incredible

[Page 167]
opportunity of observing a man riding a turtle. Rambabu latched
on and was dragged on for about a meter and a half before a flick
of the shoulders of the turtle threw him squarely into the mud.
However, those few seconds had given enough time for the rest
of the team to catch and turn her over. The whole incident was
so jaw dropping that by the time I managed to bring my jaws
back in place, Rambabu was up and we were inspecting the
injury. It turned out to be a superficial one and she was put back
on her feet and she gracefully reached the swirling waters and

disappeared into the Brahmaputra.

When the clouds cleared in the evenings, I would sit alone on the
watch tower overlooking the forest and the flooded Brahmaputra
watching the clouds chase each other over the Eastern Himalayas
across the river. Some nights I would sit alone on the terrace. The
workers would have left and Rambabu would retire to his village
after making me a flask of tea and dinner. On a clear full moon
night, the moon would rise over the dark waters flooding the
undulating grass. Elephants would emerge from the tree line as
the night wore on and graze under the tower. A rhino mother
would emerge with her baby in tow, catch the scent of a feline in
the warm breeze and hurry back into the safety of the swamps.
The stars would twinkle and I would watch the spectacle, a hot

cup of tea in hand, as contended as a man could ever be.

Then we got an invitation from the DFO Mr. R.K. Das of Dehing
Patkai Wildlife Sanctuary. Gautam and myself drove down to
Digboi town and on to Mr. Das’s residence. When we reached in
the afternoon, a pair of Oriental Pied Hornbills sat on the

coconut tree outside his office and welcomed us with their loud
calls. As we sipped our tea clouds started gathering and it started
to rain. A drive into the forest in the late afternoon was rewarded
with a herd of elephants bathing in the dense rainforest.... a
different setting from any of my previous sightings of the gentle

giants.

Four days in Dehing Patkai and it rained like Niagara. There was
hardly any let up from the pouring water. Exploring a rainforest
in rain has its own charm especially when the clouds kiss the
towering trees and the rain seeps to every pore of your body and
soul... when the puniest stream takes on the form of a raging
torrent.... when the air you breathe in tastes of moss and
lichens.... when in the pitch darkness you listen to the drumming
of the water drops on the roof of your vehicle as you feel the
presence of life all around you without seeing them.... when you

feel that you have lived life to the fullest.

.... and then you meet Dream and realise that there is so much

more to life.
