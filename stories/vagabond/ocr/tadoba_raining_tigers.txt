Tadoba: raining tigers

Just below my window, an elderly gentleman is haggling with the
price of tomatoes. The driver of a bus is having a heated
argument with a pedestrian about the right of way on the main
road and the rest of the assorted cars and buses are honking for
space. My friend Raj is waiting on the road with his son for the
school bus. The sweeper is knocking at my door. My wife has just
finished lecturing me about my eccentricities and has gone to get
the kids ready for school. How do I describe that evening in

Tadoba under such circumstances?

Blistering heat is what comes first to my mind...... blistering,
boiling, burning, April heat. Sanjeet’s Toyota Fortuner was
showing 46 degrees. The day was hot. Each breath was
laborious.... the trees and the red earth gave out the heat... it
could almost be felt. The buffer zone was our target for the day.
Our memory cards were already bursting at the seams with tiger
images as well as others from the core area of the forest. Hence
decided to explore the buffer zone. Yes, it’s true that we did not
spot any tigers but we were not after tigers either. A leopard had
mauled and killed four villagers in the buffer zone and was freely
roaming around. That is what we were after.... though we could
not have known whether it was the rogue or not in case we

sighted one.

In that blistering heat, the stripes and spots eluded us. Sambar,
wild dogs, cheetal and gaurs kept us company but overall, it was
quite uneventful though enjoyable. By the time we had reached
the forest check post in the middle of the forest check post, the
sun was getting low over the horizon. The red earth, typical of
Tadoba, looked even redder after absorbing the heat of the day. It
had rained the previous night and the grass around the pathway
still looked green in spite of suffering the onslaught of the sun for
the whole day. There was hardly any breeze. The pathway by
which we came met at the main metalled road which leads to the
Moharli Gate from Chandrapur Town and the first entrance to
the Park. The small single storied, whitewashed forest check post
stood at the cross roads surrounded by a frail looking barbed

wire fence.
The forest guards and a few village lads were playing cricket.

Yes, and this was the place where we saw a tiger crossing the
previous evening. Since the day was not very active photography
wise, we stopped our car to let our guide out and we got down
too. Vinit, Indranil and Saurabh were local lads and were
immediately summoned to the game. I joined in soon. Sanjeet,
being of a more placid nature, doubled up as a spectator cum

sports photographer. We needed some applause.

The rules and sports accessories were simple. A tennis ball, a
piece of wood resembling a bat and a plastic chair for the wicket
that was the sum total of our gear apart from the vigour and

enthusiasm. The rules were simple though not quite cricket. If

the batsman hit a ball which landed beyond a small pathway used
by the gaurs then he was out and he had the additional task of
fetching the ball. It was quite thrilling to watch the errant
batsmen looking for the ball with one eye and scanning the
surrounding forest with the other. There were no runs, since any
overthrow might send the ball into the thickets. The number of
balls faced counted as runs. The wicketkeeper sometimes shifted
the chair which replaced the wicket, from behind to bring it in
line with the trajectory of the ball. This often raised the heckle of

the batsman concerned but the game was played in due spirit.

I batted for a credible 5 runs before being dastardly bowled by
the keeper who had shifted the chair and I had raised my bat to a
wide delivery before finding that the chair was hit. After bowling
a couple of fruitless overs, I took up my position at silly mid-off
and hoped that the batsmen would not hit the ball my way. I was
having a pleasant time since hardly any balls came my way and I
had ample time to reflect on the situation. A bunch of people
ranging from fifteen to forty-five were playing cricket inside a
forest where a leopard was on the prowl but nobody seemingly
gave it a thought. A mixed herd of Cheetal and Sambar hurried
into the jungle from the left to right crossing the pathway
without even looking up towards us. A Crested Serpent Eagle
was flying high overhead in a spotless orange sky calling out
from time to time. The best part of it all was that the whole thing

seemed so natural, as if this was the most normal thing to do.

The game was on in full swing and the sun was below the
horizon when a Scorpio coming down from the Moharli Gate

screeched to a halt on the road next to our cricket pitch. I was
quite pleased at having some spectators other than our placid
Sanjeet. All showmen including players love to be watched. I
decided to pull up my socks and be a little more active in field.
Then I noticed the hands of the occupants of the Scorpio
pointing towards something and not towards us. I tried to spot
what they were pointing towards but could not see anything. The
rest of the players were lost in the game. So, I decided to find out

the cause of the excitement of the Scorpio riders.

As the Scorpio started and sped away down the road, I walked a
few meters down the road watching the forest. There was a small
water tank about 30 meters from where we were playing about 20
feet inside the jungle and sitting beside the tank was a pack of
Indian Wild Dogs or Dholes. I stood watching transfixed but
they did not get alarmed at my presence or that of the players a
short distance away. Two of them sat in the small pool of water
while the rest lazed about. There was a manual water pump
beside me and I sat on the parapet watching them. Shortly a man
came from the forest check post with a bucket. He placed the
bucket under the nozzle and started pumping water. When I
brought the pack to his attention, he gave a derisive shrug and
filled his bucket and went back. I watched the pack till it became
too dark in the undergrowth.

I walked back feeling a little satisfied... a little bewildered and

above all with a feeling of contention.

To sum up Tadoba in a few word..... tigers, lots of them, more
than you can believe.... huge open cast mines resembling the sets
of the film Avatar.... massive power plants... a herd of Gaurs just
a few kilometres away...... a man, a woman and two little children
standing at a bus stand with their meagre belongings and a
leopard crossing the road a few feet away... a forest guard on foot
controlling traffic on the road while two tigers play in front of

him.... a Gaur looking at a man answering nature’s call.

If you want to see man and animal living with each other in spite
of all the conflict of interest, come to Tadoba.
