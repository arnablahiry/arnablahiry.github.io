Paradise Regained

Tandoori paneer, gobi pakora, brandy with hot water and lemon,
crispy chicken nibbles kept appearing and disappearing.
Afterwards, for the main course, the pasta sat in a bed of cheese,
the tandoori chicken gave off an aroma that was enough to make
the most ardent vegetarian swoon, the rajma had a nice sheen of
ghee floating on top and finally when the gulab jamuns appeared,
they were the size of cannon balls but tastier.

Being a guest of the Indian Army was not an easy affair on the
digestive system and I was generously invited by Col. J.S. Rawal
of 134 Eco Task Force of the Indian Army, stationed at Nameri
for reclamation and reforestation of the buffer zone (which they
are doing very creditably, having already planted more than 2.5
million saplings against heavy opposition from local land
grabbers and militant outfits), on the occasion of his daughter's
birthday.

Since I was not expecting a dinner in the Officers’ Mess in the
middle of a jungle trip, I turned up in my jungle uniform of a
shirt, shorts and a pair of generously soiled jungle boots and sat
playing tambola and sipping brandy and hot water amid smartly
attired ladies and gentlemen. While the game was in progress
and I managed to scratch out three numbers in three different
lines on my chit of paper, a great volume of trumpeting broke
out behind the administrative building. ‘he Commanding

Officer Col. Rakesh Singh mumbled "bloody elephants" without

crankiness of wildlife photographers but I did not look back.

As I reached the electrified fence behind the administration
building, I saw their shadows passing down the jungle path.
There were five adults and a calf. They passed so close that I
could see the glint of white on the tusk of a tucker in the waning
moonlight. What took the breath away was the trumpeting, one
of them let off from time to time. I felt like standing in front of a
bagpipe as the Highland Regiment was about to charge. The
electrified fence kept us separated and they hardly paid a heed to
my tiny frame as they slowly shuffled down the jungle path
towards my bungalow which did not have the luxury of an
electrified fence and on their way to one of the adjoining paddy
fields.

The party was over by eleven at night. As the C.O. Col. Rakesh
Singh, Col. J.S. Rawal and Col. Jaggi came down to see me off at
the gate with the Unit's mascot dog, Lucky, the huge German
Shepherd, I dithered at the prospect of walking down that dark
forest road for about 200 metres to my bungalow, where the
pachyderms might be taking a snack from a roadside bamboo
clump, alone. The C.O., noticing my hesitation, graciously
offered to escort me to my residence along with the two officers.
Lucky, too behaved like an officer and a gentleman and joined
my escort.

As our party came around a bend in the path, something jumped
onto the path from the undergrowth. I shone my flashlight in its
direction. It looked like a jackal but too big to be one. Lucky did
not wait for identification. He charged straight at the supposed
jackal. Lucky was almost twice its size but it did not budge. The
pink tongue lolled and the ferocious eyes glistened steadfastly at
the charging German Shepherd. When Lucky was about a couple
of feet away, it dawned on him as well as me that his unflinching
adversary was no ordinary Jackal but the braveheart of the
jungle, the Indian Wild Dog or the Dhole, known to have
successfully attacked tigers in a pack. Lucky ground to a halt and
the two siblings separated by evolution, stood face to face sizing
up each other. Finally, brotherly relations prevailed and Lucky
luckily returned to our group. I kept shining the torch into the
eyes of the dhole till he decided to return back to his domain to

join his group which must have been inside the foliage.

Whenever I feel that there is no hope for the future of our jungles
and its inhabitants, I think about Nameri and Indian Army's Eco
Task Force and the successful battle being waged by our soldiers
for our future and hope returns once again. Those deep dark
electricity-less nights with a star studded sky and the sound of
the wind whispering sweet nothings to millions of leaves mingled
with the sound of trumpeting elephant herds, those brilliantly
fiery skies at sunset with the flocks of Red Breasted Parakeets
flying like green and pink arrows, the audible swish of the wings
of the Great Pied Hornbills as they come in to roost for the night

on the branches of an ancient tree, the mighty snow-capped
