Lost in the sands of Persia

Islamic Republic of Iran Shipping Lines (IRISL) was looking for
Radio Officers on an urgent basis. I too was looking for a change
from my previous company. We were made for each other. The
day I came across the advertisement in the Marine Club in
Bombay I popped into their Bombay agency and applied for the
job. I was assured that I shall get a favourable reply shortly. I
enquired about my status after a few days in the morning and
was asked to report in the evening. I reported in the evening and
was asked to come round in the morning. I reported next
morning and .... the “Ringa Ringa Roses” continued and it
continued for a month. The day I counted that I had just enough
money to buy me a rail ticket home and a couple of meals, I
dropped in at the office and told them that I was returning home
and to let me know over phone in case the authorities in Tehran
found my application letter and decided on anything positive.
That night I caught the Howrah Mail back home.

It was early December morning when I reached home. After
giving in to my one-year old son’s demands, since he hadn’t seen
me for over a month, for attention, I fell asleep in the mellow
winter sunshine at eleven in the morning. The phone rang in a
couple of minutes and the voice from the other end was that of
the agency in Bombay.

I reached Bombay that evening and was briefed that I shall be
travelling to Bandar Imam Khomeini via Tehran and Abadan. I
knew about Tehran and had heard about Abadan but Bandar
Imam Khomeini was unknown. Those were pre-Google Earth
days and I was quite used to travelling to unheard of destinations

and hence did not bother much.

The first hurdle came up unexpectedly at the immigration
counter in Sahar airport. When I produced my air ticket and
passport, the official thumbed through the pages and finally
asked for the visa. Now, Seamen usually don’t carry visas which
are generally granted on arrival at the airports on arrival. I
pointed this out to the concerned officer and showed him the
letter issued by the company but he was adamant that though
this was the case with most other countries, for Iran I need a visa.
Finding arguments to be futile I asked him to enquire from his

senior officer. To his credit, he took my advice.

‘We are letting you go but in case anything happens there, it will
be your responsibility’, was his tart advice to me as he handed
back my passport and documents after consulting with
somebody higher up the hierarchy. It sounded like the voice of
doom as if I was going to Pluto’s domain rather than going to
just another country. How prophetic his words were, I failed to

realise.

The aircraft was not overly crowded and there were plenty of
vacant seats but I was given the aisle seat. I hate to travel on aisle

seats but I was tired and fell asleep.

When the pilot informed over the PA that we were approaching
Tehran, I woke up and took a peek over the shoulder of my
fellow window side passenger. The scene down below was
enough to make me sit up. Jagged mountain peaks covered with
snow extended towards the horizon, the sky was so blue that it
hurt the eyes and below lay Tehran. Since, in the row opposite to
mine, there was a seat empty by the window side, I asked
permission from the airhostess to take that seat so as to get a
better look outside. When I asked her, the response startled me
since her face showed all the emotions of horror as if I had asked
something very indecent of her. However, further questioning
revealed that since there was a lady sitting in the same row, the
good airhostess had doubted my intentions. When I made my
intention a little clearer, I was allowed to take another vacant seat
next to the window. Tehran in the late morning light looked

lovely indeed from above.

As I came out of the international terminal, the agent at Tehran
was waiting for me to arrange for my transfer to the domestic
airport. The domestic airport of Tehran was a revelation. It
resembled V.T. Station of Bombay during peak hours. There
were people running helter-skelter, families who did not get a
chair in the lounge had arranged themselves comfortably on the
floor and were having their meals. Mothers shouted at their
children, grandpas and grandmas quarrelled, the men argued

By the time my flight was announced, the sun had gone down
and it was evening. The aircraft which was supposed to take me
to Abadan looked anything but airworthy and of a vintage Soviet
make. However, having some knowledge of working with
Russian equipment on various ships, I knew that they were not

was broken and just managed to stay fastened. As the Pilot
revved up the engine, the aircraft shuddered as if suffering from
acute malaria. As it picked up speed, I felt that the wing will
come apart at the seams. After an agonizing run it finally defied

gravity and was airborne.

It was almost eight in the evening when the lights of Abadan

Shortly the lights of Abadan vanished behind us and the road

sleeping pill. My eyes kept on shutting and opening.

After about an hour and a half of driving, our cab started
slowing down. I got up and looked out of the window, expecting
to see the port of Bandar Imam Khomeini. There was pitch

Sometime later, shouting broke out inside the old building.
Some heated altercation was going on inside. I sprinted across

sheepish grin and legged it.
