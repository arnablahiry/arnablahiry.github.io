Rhino and the jumping lady

It was stunning to see an old lady wearing a saree, washing plates
on the ground to jump a couple of feet from the ground from a
sitting position, onto a balcony while an irate rhino was charging
by.

I was in the outskirts of Pobitora Wildlife Sanctuary, setting up a
bird observation station just after the monsoon. Pobitora has a
surprising variety of resident woodland birds apart from the
hordes of migratory ones which visit every year during winter.
Apart from the fairly common barbets, parakeets, White-
breasted Waterhens, Lesser Adjutant Storks, various
woodpeckers and raptors, we also have the exquisitely beautiful
Scarlet-backed Flowerpeckers, the exotic Oriental Pied Hornbills,
the rare Great Mynas and many more as residents. It was my
primary objective to raise awareness about the avian diversity
present in this beautiful little sanctuary among the people

visiting this place.

Pobitora is surrounded on all sides by villages and Guwahati is
just about an hour and a half driving distance. It is more famous
as the place with the highest density of wild rhinos on the planet
and they hog the limelight. There is a healthy population of Wild
Buffaloes too but the rhinos are the species of eminence. For
years they are thriving in the wonderful grasslands that dot the
small sanctuary and the Forest Department have done a

commendable job protecting them from poachers with the help
of a willing section of the locals who proactively keep the
sanctuary clean in spite of the immense pressure from picnickers,

cattle grazers and some fishermen.

The previous night was a full moon one and I had lazed out in
the open field, soaking in the bright moonlight which was more
intense because of the clear night after the monsoon clouds had
blown away. Dazed by the sound of crickets and the light breeze,

I stayed awake later than that was prudent.

My friend Sougata was fast asleep when I woke at eight in the
morning. The sun was already up and about but waking Sougata
is a difficult proposition after a late night. I left him snoring
under the whirring fan and made my way out to have a cup of tea
with my friend Homen in the lawn under the shade of a tree of
our resort. A high road runs right next to the lawn and skirts a

part of the sanctuary.

The sun beat down and the shade of the tree was extremely
pleasant and I had a couple of sips when suddenly pandemonium
broke loose. A crowd of people started running along the road
towards the adjoining field which borders the sanctuary,
shouting at the top of their voices. A Forest Department Gypsy
screamed across and came to a screeching halt nearby and guards
armed with rifles spouted from every nook and corner
brandishing them threateningly. I dropped my cup onto the
ground and Homen, who was sitting on a branch, promptly fell

down. Everybody was shouting “rhino rhino”.

Homen ran towards the crowd while I ran into our room and
rudely woke up my snoring friend with nice bit of shaking and
told him to grab the camera and follow me. To his credit,
Sougata did not ask why or when and shot out of the bed,

grabbed his camera and scooted behind me.

When we reached the guards and the crowd, the rhino was
already on the road. He had been foraging in the paddy fields on
the other side of the road which was outside the boundary of the
sanctuary and perhaps dallied a bit too long and failed to notice
that it was already late. Now the road and the field lay between
him and the safety of the sanctuary. A few cars, cycles and
motorbikes were already plying on the road. He threw caution to
the winds and crossed the road right in front of us and the
crowd. Sougata threw himself on the ground and managed to get
a shot before the rhino descended onto the field.

Now this field was once a swampy lowland but over the years
people had bought the land and built walls and houses scattered
all around. The rhino, already furious because of the shouting
from the crowd now found it difficult to navigate around the
pillars. A cow was peacefully grazing and the rhino found that
hard to digest. He charged the cow and it ran for its life. After
wandering around for a while, the rhino found the narrow road
which runs by the side of the river across which the sanctuary
started. That river is easily forded by rhinos and we heaved a

collective sigh of relief and returned to our tea.

Soon I was standing next to the gate of our resort having a fresh
cup of tea. This area is surrounded by village homes and bamboo
groves. Suddenly somebody fired a rifle shot from next the wall
of the gate a few paces away. The cup left my hand along with the
tea as I jumped a few feet off the ground. On landing on terra
firma, I stepped outside the gate only to see the rhino break cover
from the adjoining bamboo grove a few feet away. He crossed the
narrow village road in front of me. Opposite is Homen’s house
and an old lady was sitting in the courtyard cleaning the dinner
plates from the previous night. Having heard the gunshot behind
her she turned around while in a sitting position only to see the
fence of the house being uprooted by the horn of an extremely
agitated charging rhino. In the blink of an eye the old lady
jumped from that awkward position onto Homen’s balcony
while the rhino veered and thundered across the courtyard and
into the thicket behind Homen’s house followed by a horde of

Forest Department personnel.

A few hours later we happily learned that the staff had
successfully guided the rhino back into the sanctuary and peace

again reigned in our little village.
