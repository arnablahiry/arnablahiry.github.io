Love and football in Mexico

The port of Guaymas is located on the western coast of Mexico.
Surrounded by barren mountains, the small port town is quite
picturesque. M.V. Evpo Ageos sailed in on a September morning
just as the hills were losing the early morning glow. The little
town lay placidly hemmed in between the blue bay and the

brown knolls.

The clearance was given with utmost efficiency and we started
discharging our cargo. The speed at which the cargo was being
discharged was quite surprising. Long lines of trucks hummed
around the ship. It resembled a beehive where the ship was the
queen bee and the trucks came and went like the workers. The
port resounded with the sound of trucks revving their engines,
the vessel’s derricks whined as they manoeuvred the cargo from
the cargo holds to the trucks, bulldozers and wove in and out of
the trucks gathering any spilt cargo, stevedores argued with the
workers, the deck officers argued with the stevedores over
discharging procedures and an air of brisk activity was felt

everywhere.

That evening I went out to the town. A small Mexican town has
a charm which cannot be equalled by any other. The white
washed buildings stood in perfect harmony under the
streetlights. The neat narrow lanes were humming with men,
women and children as if the whole town was out for a party.

[Page 64]
Sombreros and moustaches jostled with tight fitting jeans and
loose tops. Cars passed with people sounding the horns and
playing saxophones simultaneously. In some parks there were big
crowds listening to impromptu bands belting out sentimental
Spanish songs. The smell of tortillas and tacos wafted in the
warm breeze. Children ran around in the parks laughing and

shouting.

That night when I was returning to the ship full of pizza thickly
smeared with cheese and generous helpings of ham (none of the
fancy Pizza Hut or Dominos overpriced ham flakes) and jalapeno
and baked in a proper oven, I felt a twinge of remorse that we
would be leaving this paradise soon..... at the most another day or
less since the discharging was going at a fast rate.

I fell asleep listening to the sound of the heaving derricks. When
I opened my eyes the next morning the silence was all pervasive.
There was no sound except for the one coming from the ship’s
generators in the engine room. I looked out of the porthole... the
derricks were lying lifeless on the deck. The port which was
swarming with men and machinery the previous night was as
deserted as the Gobi Desert. When I met the duty officer who
was the 3rd Officer, he told me that around five in the morning
during Chief Mate’s watch all the workers just left the vessel in a
hurry and the trucks departed. The stevedore’s representative
also departed in a hurry without any explanation. So here we
were with the ship still with cargo in a Mexican port without

knowing what to do or where to go.

Around mid-day a car sped down and halted at our gangway.
The agent ran up and informed the Captain that a revolution has
broken out somewhere and all work had to be stopped since the
trucks could not get through. Having delivered this piece of news

he departed promptly as if in a hurry to join the revolution.

So that was that.... we were stuck in Guaymas and we could do
nothing about it. We fortunately had enough supply of fuel to
run the generators and enough food and water to last about
fifteen to eighteen days. Nobody really minded the total lack of
activity. The duty officers manned their stations during their
hours of watch. The crew did their regular maintenance jobs. By
five in the evening everything stopped and only the duty officer
and engineer manned the vessel. The rest of us were out playing
football.

Since the jetty was deserted and we were the only ship
alongside, we had a free run of the port. At five in the evening
almost the full complement of officers and crew descended on
the jetty. I had bought a football from Guatemala. Two portable
lightweight goalposts were fabricated in the engine room. Nets
were woven out of nylon lines used for passing the ship’s ropes to

tugs.

The football game was the high point of daily activity.
Everybody looked forward to the game. Even the Captain, who
was well advanced in years, agreed to act as the referee. The
glistening blue bay surrounded by the bare hills acted as a
stunning backdrop to the deserted jetty which acted as our

[Page 66]
stadium. However, any spectator coming down to watch the
game would have been shocked at the spectacle of a bunch of
sailors from Greece, India, Burma playing football in immaculate
shorts and jerseys with a portly old Greek gentleman rolling
about with a whistle in his mouth which was our Captain
Vrassidas Georgakis. Among all these sportsmanlike footballers
there was one footballer prancing about in his underwear. That

was the Bosun.

The spectator would have to watch for some time to understand
the reason behind this particular act of depravity. At some point
of time an over enthusiastic kick from one of the players would
result in the ball sailing across the parapet of the jetty and land in
the sea. Our Bosun was an ex diver from the Navy. It was his job
to dive into the water to retrieve the ball. Then he would throw
the ball to us from the sea, swim across to the other side of the
ship and climb up the gangway and descend from the shore side
gangway and re-join the fray like a dripping stocky mermaid
without fins. This went on well for quite a few days till somebody
disagreed on some finer points of the game with the Bosun and
in a fit of fury threw his brand-new Lee jeans into the water after
which, the Bosun refused to take part in retrieving the ball

anymore and the game came to a halt.

The weather was decidedly hot when we arrived in Guaymas. It
remained so when the game was being played. It was during one
of these days when I had gone ashore in the evening after a
particularly disastrous match where I had scored a same side goal
through no fault of mine. I had deftly passed the ball to the

75

[Page 67]
goalkeeper without noticing that the goalkeeper was emptying
his bladder into the sea behind the goalpost. The goalkeeper, to
his credit, was keeping an eye on the ball and helplessly watched
it cross the goal line. Later on, when an irate 3rd Engineer, who
was the Captain of my team confronted me, I cleverly pointed
out that my goalkeeper should have been in position to receive
my pass instead of polluting the sea but the goalie vehemently

defended his right to de-ballast and there was a stalemate.

Be that as it may, I was in a despondent mood when I reached
the town. All the running, arguing and the walk to the town had
made me thirsty. There was a fruit juice shop opposite a park and
the pineapples and oranges looked appealing enough to pay a
visit. I entered and reached the counter when I was hit by the

smile of Alma.

She was a diminutive girl who manned the counter or rather,
womanned the counter. Her five feet frame was lit up by a smile
which could have launched a thousand ships. Mexican ladies are
in general, extremely pretty but she was the prettiest thing I had
seen in twenty-five years of my existence at that time. Her brown
long hair framed a perfectly oval face which became luminescent
when she smiled and she greeted me with that smile and a perfect
voiced ‘hola’. Cupid released his arrow. That perfectly petite
slightly upturned nose, liquid bluish green eyes and a well-

endowed figure made sure that the arrow hit hard.

1 did not know any Spanish during that time and usually got

along fine by doing mimics to talk to people who did not know

[Page 68]
English. My mimic deserted me and I think I stood gaping
without knowing exactly why I came inside. She said something
which bypassed my ear. I would not have understood anyway but
in my state of stupor she might have said something in Bengali
and still I would not have listened. Sensing my awkwardness, she
picked up a pineapple and dangled it in front of my eyes and I
came back to my senses. I nodded my head in agreement and was
shortly seated a few meters from her holy presence with a big
glass of pineapple juice. This was followed with a few other
glasses of other fruit juices till I ran out of money. When I
walked back to my ship I was in a state of daze.

Next afternoon I just clenched my teeth and somehow managed
to finish the game of football before rushing back to my cabin to
get dressed. I do not remember exactly how I reached the juice
shop but I am sure I was floating on air. I entered the shop and
that smile hit me anew right in the middle of the solar plexus.
This time I had come with enough money to last me through half
a tropical fruit shop but alas my stomach could not hold more
than four jumbo glasses. This time I picked up her name from
her conversation. Alma. Later I got to know her full name. Alma

Hernandez.

Next evening, I was back and this time she did not even ask me
what I wanted. Pineapple juice... followed by grape.... another
glass of orange and lastly a glass of strawberry milkshake. She
placed them one after the other as I sipped and each time that
smile broke my heart in a thousand pieces. In between attending
to her customers, she shot me glances and each time | reeled as if

[Page 69]
thunderstruck. She even came up and said something in Spanish
and I just grinned sheepishly without daring to talk lest she be

riled by my absence of language skills.

When we had arrived in Guaymas, the weather was hot.
However, within a week of us getting stuck the mercury took a
steep dive. Suddenly the evenings started feeling chilly and a
jacket had to be worn. Bosun still leapt into the water with the
alacrity of a Gannet diving after a tuna. This was prior to his loss
of his Lee jeans. The game was still being played with vim.
However, the physical necessity of drinking juice from seven to
nine every evening diminished drastically. However, cupid kept
up his relentless barrage of arrows and I dutifully arrived at
Alma’s counter every evening and departed around nine like a
man possessed. She took all my dumb adulation with ease and
never showed signs of picking up one of the heavy steel fruit
mashers and bringing it down on my head. Rather she tried to
speak to me a few times but still Spanish eluded me. After
downing innumerable glasses of pineapples, strawberries, grapes
and oranges liberally dosed with ice over a period of about 4 days
after the chill set in I went down with a severe bout of bronchitis.
Getting sick on a ship is never a pleasant experience to say the
least what with no Doctors and the man in charge of cargo
operations i.e. the Chief Mate or the man in charge of navigation
ive. the 2nd Mate doing the duties of a doctor by referring to a
medical book.

Being alongside I availed of the luxury of consulting a doctor.
Only problem was that he was Mexican. Hence our exchanges

were made in English from my side and Spanish from him. After
the ‘consultation’ we shook hand departed with mutual
appreciation of our linguistic skills and precious else. True that
he gave me some medicines but since I did not know when or
how much to partake of the medication, I prudently desisted
from having any. As such I was quarantined for the next two
days to my bunk in my cabin. On the third day I was back at the

Juice shop.

There was no sign of Alma and an old wizened gentleman had
taken her place. Fortunately, to the credit of the old gentleman,
he knew a smattering of English. He informed me that Alma had

taken the day off. I returned to the ship a dejected man.

The Chief Mate Agnel Pereira, was from Goa. He was in the
South America run for most of his sailing career. He saw me
smoking a lonely cigarette at two in the morning in the Aft deck.
To his persistent queries I finally blurted out my secret. He was

most sympathetic to my cause.

He nodded his balding head in sympathy. ‘Ask her when you

meet her tomorrow donde estaba usted ayer?’

“What does that mean? Promise me that I will not be bashed up
if I say that to her’, I was a bit hesitant still not sure that the Mate

was not pulling one on me.

‘Listen, I have spent half my life in these places. I know my

Spanish.... go ahead and ask her’.

So, the next day, armed with my first Spanish sentence, I
dutifully turned up at her shop and there she was, standing in all

her radiant beauty, dispensing orange juice.

‘Donde estaba usted ayer?’, I blurted out as soon as she caught
my attention. Having said what I had to say, I was about to duck
behind the counter to avoid getting slapped when she smiled the
most heavenly smile and told me something in Spanish. The old
gentleman of the previous evening was hanging around nearby
and he came to my rescue. He translated Alma’s Spanish. It
turned out that I had asked her that where she had been
yesterday. Presumably she had some urgent shopping and had
taken the day off. She chatted with me through the old man and I
probably downed a few more glasses of various juices than my
usual quota. She came to see me off at the door of the shop

during closing time.

I walked back or rather, floated back, to my ship in ecstasy.
Chief Mate was doing something at the gangway and I reported
my progress. He was delighted and promised to accompany me

the next day. Then he hit on a brilliant idea.

“Well, 2nd October is coming up day after tomorrow. Ask her
out for dinner on that day, since it is your birthday’, he told with

the gleam of a veteran of many battles in his eyes.

‘Tcan’t...... I don’t understand Spanish and she does not
understand English.... and if you think that I am going with that
old gentleman in tow for a date, forget it’.

After a ponderous pause the Mate volunteered to accompany
me. ‘I can go as your interpreter, considering that it was me who
broke the ice between you two. However, you shall have to foot

the bill provided she agrees to go.”

I agreed with alacrity. The next day evening the Mate and I
arrived at the holy place and promptly Agnel started a
conversation. He spoke rapidly in Spanish and repeatedly
pointed towards me. I hoped fervently that he was saying
something nice about me. She looked at me and giggled which

almost made me swoon. She had agreed.

The next 48 hours were spent in agonizing ecstasy. I probably
lost a few pounds in weight too. Finally, the date and time
arrived. Accompanied by Agnel, I reached a little before closing
time. Agnel suggested that we hit a disco (apparently Alma loved
to dance like any other Mexican) before dinner to work up an

appetite and we walked to the nearest night club.

Now I love to watch dancing to a certain extent but my
expertise in the practical field is extremely limited to say the
least. If I had tried, I could have given Fred Astaire a run for his
money but I seem to have been born with two left feet. Moreover,
Iam slightly allergic to high decibel levels and this is what hit me

like a sledgehammer as soon as we entered the club.

A huge stage was lit up with psychedelic lights and the music
that was blaring out was enough to wake the dead. People were
dancing in frenzy and | reeled at the prospect of dancing in that

melee. However, with a bit of prodding from Alma and Agnel I
stepped on the floor. My first manoeuvre caught somebody in
the shin bone. I smiled apologetically but was soon prodded in
the midriffs by a hefty elbow. Next moment I trod on Alma’s toes
and ran out of steam. The only way out was to get a couple of
stiff ones to patch up my shattered morale. I left the dance floor

while the mob kept on their movements. Alma kept on dancing.

I walked up to the bar like a Bedouin walks up to the nearest
oasis. The bartender immediately understood my need for a
strong one and before long I was staring down at a shot glass
containing a clear fluid and slice of lemon perched on the rim. I
was sure that I was not served water and presumed that it must
be vodka or white rum but such a small quantity surprised me. I
was waiting for some water when the bartender asked me to
drink up straight from the glass. Hmm.... so, this must be
something different and I am always game for something
different. So, I raised the glass and gingerly took a sip. The liquid
marched down my throat spreading fire all through my
oesophagus and I choked. A kindly gentleman saw me in distress
and beckoned me to lick on the lemon and then a bit of salt on
the rim. I did as directed and immediately felt better. Tequila
started the magical work straight away and I ordered one more

shot.

Finishing the second shot started changing my outlook and the
noise seemed heavenly. Alma waved at me and I waved back a
carefree hand and ordered a third. After the third I do not
remember much but was later informed that I had ordered a

couple of more. The rest was dizzy and I came back to my
senses... or whatever was left of it, the next morning staring at the
ceiling of my cabin. Agnel told me that it was fortunate that I did
not weigh much (many years back, mind you) and the crew did
not have much trouble hauling me up the gangway on our
return. Alma had a blast and we did have some dinner though I
do not remember much of it. Agnel had footed the bill while I
half slept in a Tequila laden dream. She was dropped off at her

home and then both of us had returned to the ship.

The most devastating part was that, while I was busy in the juice
centre and then with dancing and getting introduced to Tequila,
the chaps who were conducting the revolution had run out of
steam and had withdrawn dastardly. The unloading was
underway in full swing with double the speed. We had to sail out
that evening for Ecuador and then on to Nigeria. There was no

way I could go ashore anymore.

At seven that evening, the moon rose over the Bay. Evpo Ageos
cast away all her lines and sailed out never to go back to that
magical small town where a young sailor had for the first time
lost his heart. The lights of the city diminished and the moonlit
water broke into thousands of fragments like a heart shattered by

Tequila.
