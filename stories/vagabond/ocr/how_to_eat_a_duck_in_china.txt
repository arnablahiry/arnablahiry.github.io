How to eat a duck in China

It was a duck all right and it was also true that we wanted to eat

one but this was too much.

It was the first of many visits to the port of Nanjing in China.
The mighty Yangtse Kiang meandered its way but at certain
points, looked like Champs Elysees during rush hour. Nearing
Shanghai, the river teemed with floating objects of every
description. Big freighters jostled for space with tiny boats
catching fish. Puny tugs chuffed and puffed pulling barge loads
of coal with all their might. Cross river ferries whirred and
weaved their way through the commotion as fibre glass patrol
boats zig zagged over the muddy surface. The countryside
changed from huge, smoke belching chimneys to sublime green
paddy fields as my ship M.V. Pratapgad gently eased herself
through the throng.

The port of Nanjing is about two hundred kilometres upstream
and took us more than two days to reach since our old lady could
not muster much speed and the traffic did not encourage
speeding. As the lines were made fast ashore, the floodlights of

the port came on. It was evening time.

Soon hordes of important looking officials in splendid uniforms
descended on the ship. ‘hey were from the police, immigration,

health, customs, guards, stevedores and god knows what other

departments. The small mess room cum conference room was
overflowing and it was difficult to hear above the various sounds
emitted by the horde. Subratada, my senior was in charge of
dealing with all the port officials and going by the number of
cigarettes he was smoking (those were the days when cigarette
smoking was considered an essential part of any on board
dealings), it was apparent that he was finding it hard going.
Captain D’Souza had given up and left everything to Subratada
and myself and was avidly watching a local news channel on TV,
studiously ignoring the various demands of the officials. I tried to
be helpful as best as I could by passing the staplers and various
stamps only to be rebuffed in Chinese. Probably, they needed
water and paper clips but the total lack of English in
communications proved to be a hindrance. However, matters
resolved by themselves and after about two hours of furious
stamping, gesticulating, signing and smoking our vessel was
cleared for operations. Then all the officials departed and all
operations ceased. There were no workers in sight to load our

cargo.

That did not matter either to Subratada or myself. As soon the
gangway (the staircase of the ship which connects it to land) was
secured properly we walked down and landed on Chinese soil
armed with our shore passes. The port was not too big and we
walked past containers and cranes and came up to the main gate.
The impressive guard with his impassive face asked for the
passes. As Subratada handed them out, he hummed out a tune

from the film Haqeegat which caused a twitch in the facial

muscles of the guard indicating that he was appreciative of music

Outside the gate was a highway and across the highway were
paddy fields. The sun had already set. The highway had very little
traffic and as we walked down, we saw a few shacks dotting the
roadside selling various eatables. Since we did not have any local
currency i.e. the Yuan, we turned back but not before taking a
longing look at various portions of meat hanging by the hooks at

the shops.

Next day went by and cargo operations started in earnest. The
derricks (ship’s cranes worked by means of steel ropes and
pulleys with hydraulic motors) whined as ifin their death throes.
Pratapgad had derricks which made enough noise to raise the
dead. She was an old lady and not only was showing her age but
making it heard too. To top the screeching, she spouted
hydraulic oil from her numerous leaks in the motor almost on a
bi-hourly basis like a fountain and the Batti Saab (ship’s electrical
officer who has been given this simple title universally on Indian
ships signifying that he changes bulbs or batti in Hindi. The
English version is Lecky) had to run around with a bucket in
hand to collect the spouting oil. However, in spite of all her

drawbacks, she could load and sail.

The day was hot and sultry. The Yangtse lazily flowed past.
Pratapgad had no air-conditioning which is unbelievable in this
day and age. Of course, in my cabin, | had the luxury of a small
table fan fixed on the bulkhead (that’s what a wall is called in a

ship) and there was one in the Radio Room too. Throughout the
day it sent forth gushes of warm air soaking my body in sweat.
The open forward porthole (window in shipping terms) did not
help much. By late afternoon, thirst had built up to

unmanageable levels.

As Subratada and myself walked and sweated down the
gangway, the sun was setting fire to the Yangtse but the on the
other side of the mighty river the sky darkened perceptibly and
we thought we noticed a few flashes that warmed our hearts. As
we walked up to the guard, Subratada gave another rendition of
Hageegqat and this time the guard gave a positive twist to his

facial muscles and waved us on.

This time the landscape was more visible than the previous
evening since the sun was still on the horizon. The green paddy
fields stretched away across the road and disappeared amidst
lush green hillock. Small huts lay among the paddy fields
surrounded by tall trees which had started to sway having caught
the freshly gushing breeze. As we walked down the road, the dark
clouds climbed higher in the horizon and the breeze had the

fresh earthy smell and the promise of rain.

The small roadside shacks were already open for business.
Actually, they were small residential houses where the front
portions were converted with the aid of a few rickety tables and
chairs into eateries. Nothing fancy, no decorations, you had your
chair, table and food. ‘I'he food was on display hanging from

hooks at the entrance. There were various vegetables, sauces and

different types of meat. Among the vegetables, the easily
identifiable were potatoes, green onions, spinach, carrots and
great quantities of radish. Green onions and potatoes are a
delicacy at our homes and were the natural choices. Sauces
looked delicious with their vibrant colours. However, when it
came to the meats, we were stumped. Only the chicken and duck
were familiar. There were big lumps which could have been beef
or lamb but were looking a bit shrivelled. Still others looked
suspiciously like rodents and though none of us are really finicky
about the cooked stuff, seeing the raw material was a bit
unnerving. Since chicken is more of a hoi polloi staple, we
decided to go gastronomic and ordered duck. The procedure for
ordering was very simple in the absence of any common
language. It was just a matter of pointing out the raw material
and we pointed out the potatoes, green onions and a duck
hanging from the hook and pulled our chairs outside under an
overhanging plastic cover and sat back. The beer was brought out
without us having to point them out. We were seamen and we
must have beers, that was the unwritten law. We did nothing to
dispel the perception. The bottles were ice cold and we were
sweaty. The owner of the shop served up a few delicious fritters
of something or the other and we basked in the glow of Hindi

Chini bhai bhai.

Delicious smells emanated from the kitchen. The sun set, the
clouds thundered and the frogs in the adjoining fields took up
the cue. The dim yellow lights of our restaurant cast more

shadows than light. ‘I'he paddy fields darkened and the wind

shook the green stalks and the trees with more vigour and then
the rain started. The earthy smell of the fields mingled with the
smells from the kitchen and the beer tasted awesome. It grew

quite dark.

The first dish arrived. A bowl heaped with steaming green
onions and then another one with steaming potatoes. They were
cooked separately. We did not exactly expect the cooking to be
what was made at home with spices but a separate plate of
steamed green onions and another of potatoes was unexpected.
However, the smell was great and appetizing. Then the problem
arose. How do you eat with a chopstick? I have always been in
awe of my Chinese friends in school when I saw them eating rice
with two pieces of stick and felt that some type of magic must be
involved somewhere. Now was my turn. This was my first trip to

China. There were no spoons.

Seeing our dilemma, the shopkeeper ventured to give a
demonstration. It was quite simple, hold one stick diagonally
against your palm with the stick resting between the crook of the
thumb and the ring finger. With the index and middle finger
hold the other stick and manoeuvre both the sticks to hold the
food and pop it into your mouth. The first few attempts led to
three tiny pieces of green onion and half a piece of potato
reaching my mouth. The rest scattered far and wide. Subratada
fared no better. However, with more attempts, we found that an.
acceptable quantity was reaching our mouths and the taste was
out of the world. I have no idea how the magician in the kitchen
managed to just steam two vegetables and attain such heights of

[Page 125]
culinary magnificence. I always thought that the Chinese were
magicians but now I knew. Then came the duck.

There was no pre announcement of its arrival. As we sipped the
beer and munched on the green onions, the plate was placed on
our table. On the plate lay a whole duck. Yes, a whole duck
without the feathers. The yellow beak was clamped shut, the
webbed feet were neatly tucked under the bloated belly and the
eyes stared without seeing. The skin had goose bumps all over.
The whole bird was plucked of the feathers, the intestines cleared
out and then was boiled. The potato that I was about to gulp got
stuck in my gullet while Subratada spluttered on his beer. The
shopkeeper showed concern but the duck looked on placidly. I
blinked but the apparition did not go away.

The shopkeeper was asked to take it away and cook it properly
and bring it back. He did that promptly and shortly returned
with the same duck but now it was sitting on a bed of cucumbers.
We, the Chinese and us Indians, were neighbours but language

was not the best thing we shared.

Finally, Subratada picked up the duck by the throat and
marched into the kitchen, followed by the shopkeeper, myself
and the full household of the shopkeeper. Subratada cut the duck
in small pieces, discarded the head and feet and fried the whole
thing in oil. In the absence of chilies, half a bottle of chili sauce
was emptied into the pot which shook our hosts who gave out a

lot of ‘oohs’ and ‘aahs’. Finally, the meal was ready. ‘his time we

By the time we finished the last morsel of duck and washed it
down with the last gulp of beer, we had mastered the art of
handling the chopstick. As we ambled back towards the ship,
along the dark road with rain pitter pattering on our shoulders
and the beer sloshing around nicely in our tummies, the frogs
croaked and cheered in unison at our achievement on having our
first Chinese meal albeit cooked by Subratada, on Chinese soil
and mastering the sticky art of using sticks.
