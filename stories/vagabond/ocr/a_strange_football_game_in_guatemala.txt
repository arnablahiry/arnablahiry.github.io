A strange football game in Guatemala

The brilliantly coloured Quetzal is the name of the national bird
of Guatemala as well as the name of its currency. The port of
Puerto Quetzal is on the Pacific coast of Guatemala in Central
America. My ship, M.V. Evpo Ageos arrived there one July
evening in the year 1994 from Tampa, USA. Out of the crew and
officers numbering twenty-eight, we were three Indian Officers
and two Indian Cadets and the fitter Maruti Narayan Parker was
Indian. Other than myself, the Chief Engineer Mr. Sampath and
Chief Officer Mr. Pereira were Indians. Rest of the crew was an
eclectic mix of Greeks, Cypriots, Burmese and one German. This
floating United Nations had one of the best crowds I have ever

had the pleasure of sailing with.

We reached Puerto Quetzal quite late, around two in the
morning. When the usual port documentation was completed
and I finished reporting to the Head Office, I popped outside to
get a breath the of fresh air. The sky had a wonderful and strange
reddish hue. Apparently one of the volcanoes was in flow.
Sometimes a distant staccato noise could be heard. The stevedore

told us that a local civil war was in progress but quite far away.

Next evening Bara Saab (that is what the Chief Engineers are
universally called by Indians) Mr. Sampath, Maruti Narayan
Parkar the fitter and I went ashore. Fortunately, we found a cab
just outside the gate. Since, after tasting ship’s food, which tasted

like cooked blotting paper after a few days, for quite some time,
we directed the cabbie to take us to a restaurant. The cab weaved
through dark roads with dense trees standing on both sides
interspersed with small villages. There were a couple of army
checkpoints with smart soldiers holding smart looking guns who
demanded to see our shore passes with granite expressions but
relented on seeing that we were Indians and not one of the

guerrilla fighters.

The crab drew up alongside a small eatery by the roadside
where we were served a fiery and chunky Prawn Veracruz with
various vegetables and some delicious small bread with some
stuffing which melted in the mouth. After the meal we proceeded
to the town. Most of the shops had closed but one sports shop,
which was about to close, had a football (not the American type)
displayed at the shop window. On the spur of the moment, I
decided to buy it. The rest of the town was quite non-descript
and had a faint dilapidated look about it.

When the cab dropped us back at the gate, it was past ten thirty.
Just as we entered the gate, the heavens opened up as only can
happen in a tropical country, It was not a downpour but more
like a waterfall. The water blotted out everything else.
Fortunately, the awning on top of the gate was enormous and
covered quite a bit of area and after showing our pass to the

sentry we stood under the shade waiting for the deluge to stop.

‘The rain had no intention of complying with our wishes and

instead increased in ferocity. Great droplets crashed on the thick

foliage outside the gate. Thunder rolled continuously. The
awning under the gate stood like a small dry island in the middle
of the deluge. The flood lights illuminated the raindrops and the
leaves of the dense trees around the gate, giving them a strange
phosphorescent appearance but could hardly penetrate the liquid
wall. Returning to the ship through that waterfall was out of

question. So, we decided to wait it out.

Time passed by but there was no let up. The guards behind their
sand bags and machine guns lounged around and smoked. Three
of us sat chatting and looking at the rain. It was getting close to
midnight. I suddenly remembered the football in my hand and
took it out of the bag. Both Bara Saab and Parkar were well
advanced in years and so I started to juggle the ball with my feet,
alone. I used to play a bit of football before I joined Merchant
Navy and was an average left wing back. I have no doubt that I
could have played for Mohun Bagan or at least Real Madrid if I
received encouragement from my parents but that, alas, was not

to be and the world lost a great wing back.

As had happened often before, I lost control of the ball while
juggling and the ball shot off and landed in one of the sand
bagged sentry post. I half expected the sentry to turn the gun,
which looked more like a canon, towards me and let loose a
couple of volleys but the sentry and apparently all other sentries
were watching me while I was practicing my skills. Now he came
out of his sand bagged castle and started juggling the ball himself.
In no time the ball escaped his feet and landed inside the
adjacent sand bagged citadel. 'I'here were two soldiers inside this

one and they came out and started passing the ball to each other
and to me. Ina short while all the soldiers had left their posts and
a brisk game of passing the ball ensued. The rain continued
unabated. Bara Saab and Parkar sat and watched the game from
the top of a heap of sand bags perched on either side of a
machine gun. As we got warmed up, a light came on from the
first floor of the guard tower and everybody stopped in their

tracks.

Heavy footsteps were heard coming down the stairs and shortly
a short, bald and rotund man clad in heavy boots, camouflage
shirts, an extremely brief brief and nothing else, stood glaring in
front of us. The soldiers stood around sheepishly with the one
holding the ball looking a little more sheepish than the rest. I
stood in the middle of the camouflage clad gang in my red T-
shirt and jeans looking a bit out of place. Bara Saab and Parkar
didn’t budge from their high perch and looked down at us
interestedly. Here, obviously, was the Big Boss. The glaring
continued for some time and then a surprisingly thin tone rang
out from the boss. I almost let out a laugh but, looking at the

faces of my fellow players, thought better of it.

A volley of Spanish from the boss signalled the end of the
glaring session. He kept up the blaring in that thin reedy voice of
his as the nice paunch heaved to and from with each bellow. As
laughter bubbled inside me, I wondered at the discipline of the
soldiers and admired their restraint at not rolling around

laughing. Slowly the tirade veered to the unfortunate chap

holding the ball and he replied in Spanish and pointed towards
me. The laughter inside me died a premature death.

As he came up towards me, Bara Saab and Parkar left their
perch and came running. The boss gave me a piercing look and
let loose a volley of Spanish. It was my turn to look sheepish.
When I thought that I might get shredded to pieces by the boss’s
bare hands, a huge grin split his face. He bounded across to the
soldier holding the ball, plucked it out and shot it up in the air.
There was a roar of approval as the hovering dark clouds lifted
from our shoulders. Within minutes we were divided into two
teams and even Bara Saab and Parkar were forced to join under
jovial threats with one of the soldiers pointing his bayonet at
Bara Saab’s nether region. Somebody brought out a crate of beer
from the store room. The football battle raged with generous
gulps of beer to revive any flagging spirits. The rain .... well, just
kept on raining. The strange sight of a bunch of Guatemalan
soldiers in fatigues, three Indians of varying ages in civvies and
one rotund, half naked man bouncing energetically around with
a football with a can of beer in one hand, under floodlights well
past midnight in Guatemala, while a tropical depression vented
all its fury on the jungle around, is one that will remained etched

in my memory till my last breath.

‘Jolly chaps, those’, drawled Bara Saab as he wobbled slightly
and limped up the gangway. Some energetic soul had kicked him
on the sheen with heavy steel toed boots in the heat of the battle
but the wobble might have been induced by the refreshments
provided.
