The man who bought a mountain

‘But why did he buy a mountain?’ that was the first question
which came to my mind when my cousin brother, Bubai, told me
that he had been to Mathaburu (the Bald Mountain in Saontali
dialect) the previous winter where a certain Chittababu had

bought the mountain.

‘His passion is to sculpt huge birds on the bare face of the
mountain top’, Bubai said.

‘In that case we must go and see it... if somebody has bought a
mountain for sculpting birds, the least we can do is to go and see
it in first person, before the gentleman was shipped off to the

loony bin.’

It was the month of November 1998 and we were discussing the
follies of mankind in general after a heavy lunch. Thus, a trip to
the remote parts of the district of Purulia in West Bengal was
settled. Bubai had a friend, Rajkumar, who wanted to see the
works of the modern Michael Angelo and I had a friend
Shoumitro, alias Shomu, who was interested in any sort of

madness and thus a team of four was born.

The catch came two days before departure when Bubai
announced that some friends of his had suggested that instead of
visiting Mathaburu straight away, it would be a better trip to go

toa place called Murguma, stay overnight and then proceed to

Mathaburu on foot the next day. Purulia is a hilly and forested
terrain.

‘Do you realise that I weigh a ton and have no wish to reduce it

by boulder hopping?’ I enquired quite alarmed.

“My friends have assured me that it is only half a day’s walk
from the Murguma dam and the path does not cross hills but just

skirts the lower slopes,’ Bubai said disarmingly.

The die was cast and I had no way of backing off, more so
because Shomu weighed at least half a ton more than me and was
quite enthusiastic about the short walk. Talk about peer

pressure...

Boarded the train at night and disembarked at Purulia station
next morning where the body of my brand-new Nike shoe
promptly severed diplomatic relations from its sole on my right
foot. I bought a pair of rubber slippers for twenty rupees which

incidentally lasted about a year and a half of rigorous use.

We got onto a bus bound for the hamlet of Begunkondor and
took an autorickshaw after that to reach Murguma. It was a
sleepy hamlet of a few huts. There was one shop which sold
everything from kerosene to Kentucky fried papad. Since we
were denied entry in the dilapidated forest rest house we stocked
up on kerosene and enquired about possible camping grounds
and were directed towards the towering wall of the dam. We

climbed the embankment and stared.

In front of us was a huge water body... the water had dried up
in patches, leaving small boulders and pebbles. It was
surrounded on three sides by green hills and the reddish-brown
earthen path from the village winded up through verdant
deciduous trees. The steep bank was covered with huge boulders
which we overcame and finally pitched our small tent in one of

the dried-out patches after walking through a stretch of water.

It was past mid-day and we were famished. Soon the little stove
got blazing and we prepared a delicious dish of Maggi noodles
and a few cups of tea. After our lunch Bubai and Rajkumar
returned to the village for dinner arrangements while Shomu
promptly fell asleep. I too felt drowsy as the warm late autumn
air blew across the lake creating little ripples. The sun shone
brightly on the water of the lake and the silence was time to time
broken by a Brahminy Kite calling shrilly overhead and the
mooing of a few stringy cows grazing on the other side of the
lake. Then a few pink fairies came sailing over the lake and I
shook hands with Jim Corbett behind my tent. He started telling
me a long-winded story about how he shot a goat in the jungles

of Amazon.

When Jim finished his story, I opened my eyes. Shomu sat
smoking at the water’s edge gazing dreamily out at the water. Of
Bubai and Rajkumar there was no sign. The sky had turned
bright orange. Darkness was descending. Then two figures leapt
onto the boulders from the embankment and soon the two
musketeers waded through the water clutching two bottles full of
water wearing huge grins. ‘I'he water turned out to be Mouli, a

[Page 11]
local drink distilled from the Mohua fruit. They had arranged for
our dinner which would arrive around nine in the evening.

Soon darkness descended. A small candle fought a losing battle
with the darkness from a corner of our tent, shielded from the
breeze. The bottles were opened and as the contents sank in
volume, a huge moon rose over the hills. There was not a light in
sight. The lake turned into liquid silver and the mountains sat
motionless. Only the sound of lapping ripples of water at our feet
mingled with our conversation. Even we did not dare to raise our
voice in spite of the influence of Bacchus as if afraid that the
beauty might shatter.

A gentleman carrying a lantern brought our chapattis and
mutton curry from the village and we savoured the meal sitting
under a million stars. Shomu faced a slight trouble trying to wash
his hands after the meal, from the water of the lake, since he was
taking two steps backward for every step he took forward but
that did not interfere with his snoring inside the tent when we
finally went to sleep which was only broken once by a pair of
jackals who wanted to investigate the new construction coming

up on their property.

We were supposed to start for Mathaburu by seven in the
morning. We stuck to the schedule and departed a little after
nine. The path winded pleasantly skirting the lake for about a
kilometre and a half towards the hills. After that it reared its head
like Jack’s beanstalk and headed straight up the hills. | looked at

20

[Page 12]
the inclination, horrified but there was no turning back. If looks
could kill, I would have been one cousin brother short.

The climb started and our backpacks became heavier with every
step. After about an hour and a half’s arduous climbing the path
petered out and ended in a stream. We hacked a path through
the jungle and eventually came upon a game path. This too had
no inclination of going downhill. The sun now became hotter.
Slowly the game trail widened a bit and still climbed. Water ran
out. We had not seen a soul since we had departed from

Murguma.

Just when the trail became wide enough and we were thinking
that we had reached some sort of regular track, the trail
bifurcated. Right at the bifurcation there lay a huge pile of
dung.... elephant dung. It was not very old either. The first

impulse was to run.... but which path to take?

Bubai and Rajkumar took the trail on the left while Shomu and
I stood next to the pile of dung. Then we sat down with our back
to each other to cover all the sides in case of a charge from the

pachyderms.

The jungle was absolutely still. The heat simmered and the
crickets kept up their monotonous whirr from the bushes. Not a
waft of breeze stirred the leaves of the trees. The uncanny feel

that invisible eyes were watching us, grew uncomfortably.

‘In case of a charge, fling off the backpack and run’, I spoke

under my breath, ‘and in case it does not deter the charge take off

[Page 13]
your shirt and fling it to the ground. The elephant will be
distracted by the shirt and you will get a few more seconds.’

‘Tf the elephant still charges what do I take off next?’ enquired

Shomu in a hoarse whisper. He sometimes becomes incorrigible.

Bubai and Rajkumar returned shortly in a jubilant mood. The
path on the left had a tell-tale sign of civilization.... a torn rubber
slipper draped on a lantana bush. Extremely relieved, we took the
trail on the left only to come up against another bifurcation
about half a kilometre up. This time the trail to the left led
downhill. I was fed up of acting like Tenzing Norgay and settled
for the downward trail. Soon that trail turned into a path used by
wild boars. We crawled for a few yards and then beat a strategic

retreat.

The other trail which I had disregarded finally showed us the
end of our travails when we thought that we shall have to camp
out the night in the middle of the elephant habitat. Far below use
was a hut which had signs of being inhabited. When our tired,
aching and parched bodies reached a date palm tree near the hut,
we could not find anybody. There was a muddy pond adjacent to
the hut with a dead frog floating upside down. The water
quenched our thirst after being treated by a generous dose of

water purifier and a silent prayer.

From the date palm tree, the trail widened and finally became a
human trail. There were a few bullock cart wheel tracks on the
dried mud of the trail and lo...... we met a man pushing a cycle.

[Page 14]
He gave us directions to the nearest village.... ‘you see that small
tree? Take a left turn and after sometime the road splits in three.
Take the extreme left turn and then take one more left turn from

a banyan tree.’

Another twenty minutes of weary steps following the direction
given by our friend brought us back under the same date tree
beside the hut. Since practical jokers are not very popular till they
are hundred years dead and gone, it was very fortunate that

neither the man nor the cycle was in sight.

We followed the tracks of the bullock cart and finally reached a
small hamlet. While approaching the hamlet, apparently the
whole village turned up and we entered a baked mud centre
point of a cluster of huts in a procession led by the headman or
mukhiya. He had already smilingly given his consent to our
camping inside the village after he heard that we had traversed
the hills from Murguma and had crossed the path of a herd of
elephants that frequently raided the crops of the village. Our
uncanny feeling of being watched inside the jungle might not

have been a figment of our imagination after all.

We pitched tent under a Gulmohar tree. The rest went down to
a stream to collect water and freshen up. I sat on a log and was
immediately surrounded by the whole village. The mukhiya had
gone somewhere and the rest of the population sat surrounding
me and looking up to me with expectant and amazed eyes. I
almost felt that | needed to give a speech of some sorts about the

situation of the crops or the current economic situation in

[Page 15]
Namibia. It was an extremely uncomfortable situation and I
squirmed in embarrassment till I was rescued by the rest of the

team returning. It was my turn to go down to the stream.

Everything was red. A short distance downhill from our
camping site through some patches of vegetable farms lay the
small stream flowing busily down. Huge boulders lay glistening
like prehistoric beasts. I sat down on one and dipped my feet into
the cold flowing water. The response was miraculous to say the
least. Till then I thought that I was carrying my feet and not the
other way around but the water brought life into my dead limbs.
I sat and looked around in a trance. The sun had just set and the
water was red.... the sky was red.... the boulders were red.... the
trees overhanging the stream were red.... the grass and the bushes
were red.... perhaps that was an omen (now these same places are
the bastions of the Maoists). Somewhere in the village a dog
barked. A lady from the village gave a brief scolding to some
unseen child. A Nightjar flew down and sat on a boulder and on
spotting me, tried to make itself disappear by turning into a piece
of rock. As I stared at it, it perhaps felt uncomfortable and flew
off into the bushes with a sharp chuck.... chuck.... chuckoooo and
kept repeating the haunting call at intervals. The wind, now a
little cold flew down the crevices, picked up some leaves from the
forest floor and danced a little foxtrot with them before
discarding them like a careless lover and blew on. It was fully

dark when I made my way back to the camp.

It took almost the whole of next day to reach Ayodhya Pahar,
the nearest semi-town. Rajkumar and Bubai, being fleet footed,

[Page 16]
went on ahead and Shomu and I followed in a more leisurely
pace. We walked through vast fields of mustard flowers and
small Adivasi (original inhabitants) villages. We asked direction
for Ayodhya Pahar from an old man carrying a deadly looking
axe. He wanted a bidi (local made cigar made from tendu leaves)
and was delighted when offered a cigarette instead. He grandly
informed that Mr. Jyoti Basu (former Chief Minister of West
Bengal) was a great man since he had been to Calcutta in the year
1982. He asked what my profession was and on being informed
that I work on a ship, asked me whether I was the driver or the
cleaner. I was taken to his small hut in the jungle and offered
water and puffed rice and introduced to his family. When I left
his humble abode, I was convinced that gentlemen are not a

vanishing breed.

From Ayodhya Pahar we managed to hire a vehicle which
dropped us at Mathaburu. A short walk from the main road
brought us to the foot of the mighty hill. It was almost dark.
Chittababu had constructed a small concrete house adjacent to
the jungle. Since no one was at home we started pitching camp
next to the building when a man with a lantern appeared. When
asked we informed him that we intended to spend the night in

our tent. He was shocked.

‘Elephants will descend at night. Why don’t you stay inside the
hut?’

We had thought about it but had desisted since we did not have
permission to stay in the building. Sleeping in the path of wild

[Page 17]
elephants clinched the issue and we promptly opened the
unlocked door. The man proceeded towards the jungle.

“What about you? Where are you going inside the jungle?’ we

enquired.

‘Thave a small plot of vegetable farm inside the forest. Don’t
worry, I know when they are around’ and he cheerfully
disappeared inside the gathering gloom. The feeble glow of the

lantern was soon swallowed up by the trees.

Shortly the moon rose above Mathaburu in full glory. We
looked up at the huge massif, black as coal. The colossal figures
of huge birds adorned the top of the mountain, glowing white, in
sharp contrast with the black rock. They looked as ifa flock of
prehistoric birds had frozen in time. The small patch of field was
awash in moonlight as we looked on in awe at the work of the
genius who had created such a spectacle. Chittababu will be
forgotten in the chapters of history but his eccentric creativity
will probably live long till the time we redefine the term

‘eccentric’,

The next day we returned to ‘civilization’.
