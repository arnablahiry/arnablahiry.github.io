Lepchajagat

I met them for the first time on a dark stormy night in
Lepchajagat. It was the month of September and monsoon was in
full swing across India. I had landed up in the Forest Bungalow,
the only available accommodation those days. Lepchajagat is a
small sleepy hamlet on the main road leading from Darjeeling to
Mirik via the town of Sukhiapokhri. The bungalow is located on
the brink of a steep gorge about 100 metres down the road
slightly downhill. It is totally invisible from the main road and
was almost fully surrounded by dense conifer and rhododendron
forests but has recently thinned down due to wood cutting. This
bungalow is built in a sort of cove and is frequently surrounded

by clouds though the surroundings might remain clear.

The power lines had snapped due to the rains and the bungalow
had no electricity for four or five days when I arrived. I have been
a regular visitor to this bungalow since 2007 and I specifically
chose the monsoons to avoid other guests who at times can

hinder my bird photography.

By eight in the evening Karma, the caretaker had cooked his
signature khichri and scrambled eggs accompanied by a fabulous
fresh salad and papad, served me and vanished towards the
village uphill. The cold as usual was intense and I lit my candle
and settled down in a pile of blankets on my bed. The candle
threw a diffused light and the whole building was empty except

me. The clouds thickened all around the bungalow and the large
rhododendrons behind the door dropped the moisture
incessantly. The air itself was heavy with cold and rain and the
faint candlelight that percolated through the window panes only
intensified the darkness outside. These are the types nights when
Count Dracula is on the move and for some reason or the other,
I have always loved these atmospheres. Snugly reading my
Gerald Durrell (My Family and Other Animals) in the
candlelight, my senses were brought back from the sunny island
of Corfu to the dark cloudy Himalayan night by the alarm call of
a Barking Deer quite close at hand.

As I mentioned before, Lepchajagat is quite surrounded by
forests though close to Darjeeling and the forest holds a sizeable
number of leopards and reportedly some Himalayan Black bears
too. The Barking Deer had voiced its call quite close by and I
grabbed my torch, opened the back door near the gorge and
flashed the light across the fencing towards the location of the
call. To my utter surprise and glee, the beam fell squarely on
three Yellow-throated Martens walking nonchalantly on the
fence. Though they are not a rare or threatened species yet
spotting one is quite tough due to the terrain in which they live,
mainly the Himalayas and the foothills. Spotting three together
in the dead of the night (yes... ten at night in Lepcha is definitely
dead of the night), alone, with the clouds swirling around in the

dark slopes of the mountains was exhilarating to say the list.

Since that night I tried to spot and photograph them for three
years at regular intervals without much success. A scat here... a

little fur there... some chickens missing... that was all that they
left as signatures of their presence.

One late January morning I lay flat on my stomach on the dew
drenched lawn in front of the bungalow photographing the
Himalayan birds which came to forage on the tit bits in the
garbage dump by the side of the kitchen. They came flitting in
from the dense undergrowth picked up a piece of potato or a
crumb of bread discarded by the cook, squabbled and went back
into the bushes. I clicked away to my heart's content, a lazy
winter sun shone through the mist, Karma fought a running
battle with a large slab of dough inside the kitchen and the
chowkidar gossiped with a village belle behind me outside the
gate.

All of a sudden, the birds cackled and disappeared en masse and
a small delicately beautiful dark head popped up in my camera
view finder. The alert beady and soulful eyes could melt butter at
sixty paces. The head rose up above the gradient of the lawn and
Theld my breath trying unsuccessfully not to bat my eyelid to
avoid detection. I was lying just about 15 metres from the Marten
but the eyes were not looking at me. They looked directly behind
me at the chowkidar warily and kept on looking. To his credit,
the chowkidar was more focused on the belle than on peeping

Martens.

When she was assured that there presently was no danger from
the chowkidar, she started foraging in the dump without noticing

me. Now my chance came and my camera went berserk. The

minutes flew and suddenly another head popped up... smaller,
more delicate and infinitely beautiful. The cub had joined the
mother. Both started feeding when Karma decided that the
dough had taken enough beating and decided to pop out of the
kitchen right beside the feeding Martens. I hoarsely whispered
for him to go back but Karma’s left year was almost deaf and my
cries went in vain. The mother Marten immediately reared up in
alarm, my shutter went click and the next moment mother and

child was there no more.

Since then the hamlet of Lepchajagat has expanded into a small
village with homestays. The forest bungalow has put on a fresh
coat of paint and tourists visit all the year round. There are
hardly any power cuts anymore. Food has been revamped to
cater to the urban taste. The trees have thinned having fallen to
the axe. However, still the swirling mists covers the little building
like a blanket and the hog deer still barks on lonely nights when
the spotted cat is on the prowl in the gorges. The windows still
frost up in the early mornings and the delicate magnolia still
blooms in spring. Somewhere among those enchanted mossy
pines and oaks, I hope, that beautiful little furry family and their

progeny still roam happy and free.
