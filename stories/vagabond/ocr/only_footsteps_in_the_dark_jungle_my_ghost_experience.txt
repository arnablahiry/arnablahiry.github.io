Someone kept walking up and down the gravel and boulder
“road” in the starlit night of Chandaka Dampara Wildlife
Sanctuary. Whoever it was, he/she should have been plainly

visible. He/she was not.

It is a small wildlife sanctuary very near Bhubaneshwar, the
capital of Orissa or Odisha state of Eastern India. Hardly
anybody other than bawdy college students looking for a place to
party, showed up at the gates. In my eight days in the sanctuary I
saw only two groups of tourists who had actually come to see the

sanctuary and stayed for a night.

Chandaka has two places to stay, one is a small cluster of log huts
atop a hillock and the other is the forest bungalow...a small
bungalow with two rooms. I was staying at the bungalow. It had
electricity but due to a recent storm, there was no electric supply
for a major part of the time which suited me fine. The forest was
said to be home to a herd of elephants, chital deer, rhesus
macaques, leopards and one crocodile in one of the water bodies.
There was plenty of bird life though. The sanctuary is dominated
by a huge lake. The post monsoon forest was verdant green with

the reddish forest tracks winding around the trees.

Pradeep, the chowkidar of the bungalow, cooked a packet of

There was a guard who had a little hut by the side. He had a
small patch of garden where he had planted brinjals, ladies’
fingers, and red spinach. At that particular point of time only the
red spinach had survived the onslaught of wild boars. After an
initial introduction Pradeep departed promising to pick me up
after duty hours before sundown. He had given the chowkidar

the responsibility of taking care of my lunch.
“Sir, what would you like for lunch?”
“What would you suggest?”

“Red spinach and rice”, said he expansively as if presenting me

with the menu of Raffles.
“Do you have any other option?”, I enquired.

He looked vaguely around towards his vegetable patch as if
looking for some foie-de-gras and on not finding any he
suggested some chillies which he had in stock. I agreed to his

suggestion.

I set up my camera next to the water hole and started my lone
vigil. Certainly saw a few deer and rhesus macaques but the
elephants were absent. A couple of hours later the chowkidar
prepared my rice and spinach and left in his bicycle for his home
leaving me to my own devices. The day ended uneventfully
except for the fact that the spinach tasted heavenly with the rice
and fresh chillies.

Peafowl in Chandaka

This routine was repeated for the next nine days. The elephants
stayed away from the waterhole and the monotony was broken
only once by a male monkey and a deer. The young deer was
grazing peacefully under the shade of a tree oblivious of the
monkey sitting on a low branch just above him. I sat across the
water hole lazily watching them when the monkey, with an
effortless swing vaulted across the branch and still swinging from
the branch, landed a juicy kick on the deer’s backside making
him almost lose his balance. Next moment the monkey was
sitting nonchalantly on the branch with an air of innocence
looking intently at his finger nails while the deer, having
regained his balance, glared with his beautiful liquid eyes

balefully at the criminal.

In front of me, beyond the gate lay a small fallow land and the

My Forest Rest House in Chandaka

There was no moon but the stars twinkled brightly throwing a
faint yellowish light on the path which competed with my candle.
There was no wind. Jungle fowls bade farewell to the day with
their piercing cries and retired for the night. A pair of Spotted
Owlets had their abode on a tree behind my bungalow. They
were not the happiest of couples and had quarrels almost on a
daily basis and that night was no exception. Cheeeeyaaaak
chyazak alleged probably the female. Chyak Cheeeeeeeeek
cheeeeek vehemently denied probably the male. They had bout
of about ten to fifteen seconds with a rest periods ranging from
two to twenty minutes. Jackals howled from the hillock down the

road.

Around seven thirty or eight as the wind picked up, I heard the
crunch of footsteps coming down the road. It was a bit early for
Pradeep to come with my dinner. He might be coming to fetch

something. I waited but couldn’t see the light of his lantern

which he usually carried. The crunch on the gravel came closer

surprise, no human figure materialized. The footsteps were
clearly audible and whoever it was, was moving up the path
towards the interior of the forest but of a tangible human form
there was a complete absence. As I sat dumbfounded, the
footsteps moved up the road and disappeared in the darkness of
the forest. The wind blew strong and steady, the path was still lit
by the stars, the jungle loomed dark and menacing and my
candle light flickered. Whoever the disembodied soul was, he
certainly had guts to walk along that path at that time of the

night where wild elephants roamed.
Pradeep coming with my breakfast

As I sat musing on the guts of my invisible neighbour, I heard
the steady steps of footsteps returning down the path. I heaved a
sigh of relief assuming that the owner of the footsteps had
realised the folly of walking on a forest trail at night without
lights or human body with wild elephants around and was
returning to the forest gate. This time I was ready with my torch
to see the trespasser and as soon as the footsteps reached the
front of my gate, I switched it on. The strong beam cut across the
path and illuminated nothing more than the empty path and the
trees and bushes beyond. Yet the footsteps continued down the
path. T sat down heavily in my chair but I didn’t have to sit for

long. The footsteps returned.

This time I can say without hesitation that my hand did quiver

jungle became very quiet. I felt a bit cold which I told myself was
due to the wind and not because of the figureless night walker
outside my gate. For some reason the hair at the back of my neck
kept on standing which too was due to the cold.

Pradeep returning down the path

About three quarters of an hour passed when I saw the light of a
storm lantern coming down the forest path from the gate. This

surely was Pradeep and so it was. He had brought my dinner.

“Listen Pradeep, somebody is walking up and down the path for
quite some time. Any idea who is he because I can’t see him by

the light of my torch”, my voice, I am afraid, was not steady.

Pradeep gave me a strange look. “Nobody crossed the gate since

sundown. I was at the gate.”
“Wait a few seconds and you can hear for yourself.”

Surely enough the footsteps returned. Pradeep took out his big

torch and flashed and again there was nobody. The footsteps

“Wait a bit and he will return”, I assured Pradeep. I am not sure
how much he got assured but he picked up a small, homemade
spear from his room and in the other hand held his torch and
waited at the gate. I, not having a spear, picked up a rather solid
looking piece of wood though I was not sure how to tackle a
footstep without a body with a spear or stick. However, it gave
me something to hold on to instead of just hope and with my

torch in the other hand I too waited.

The footsteps returned and both of us rushed out to the path in
unison, torches blazing, spear and stick ready. On reaching the
path we realised that the steps were coming not from the path
but from behind the trees and bushes from the side of the dam.
This was still more flabbergasting and we rushed headlong into
the thicket like a pair of miniature elephants rushing to a

waterhole.

Coming out on the other side of the thicket we probably
expected to find a spectral body probably marching down
wearing a pair of jackboots with blood dripping from the mouth
but there was nothing...absolutely nothing. The water in the vast
dam reflected back the starlight breaking into a million parts, the
tress stood dark silhouetted against the sky swaying slightly in
the breeze, the grassy knoll lay silent but of ghosts of any sort

there was an acute shortage. However, the footsteps were quite

audible in the stillness of the night and they were coming from

Our torches revealed the secret of the footsteps. There was a
row of fishing boats tied to stakes on the water’s edge. The
“beach” had taken a horseshoe shape at this place and the wind
had blown up a small ripple which travelled across the water’s
edge from one end to the other and then the same ripple
returned. The boats rose and plopped back on the water with the
passing of the wave one after the other and that sound produced
the effect of a footstep. As the boats rose and fell one after the
other from one end of the miniature bay to the other the

“footsteps” too arrived and faded till the wave returned.

My evening meal had gone cold by this time but yet tasted

delicious and the footsteps lulled me to a blissful sleep soon after.
