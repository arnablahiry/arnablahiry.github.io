Where is Perawang? Nobody had a clue. The closest landmark
we were told was the city of Pekanbaru in Indonesia. A frantic
search of the charts did not reveal the port. We were discharging
in Kakogawa in Japan and had orders to sail to Nanjing, China to
load for Perawang. There were a few Indonesian ships docked
near our vessel and a visit to one of them revealed the location.
We even managed to get a photocopy of a chart and sailed out in

due course.

Loading in Nanjing was just routine since Nanjing was almost
like our home port. We were almost sailing up and down the
Hwang Ho once every one and a half months. This time we
loaded boiler parts for some sugar factory in Indonesia. After
spending about seven days in the port of Nanjing and duly
consuming a vast array of delicious Chinese village food (the port
of Nanjing used to be in the middle of a village by the river,
surrounded by vast paddy fields and small villages) and Tsingtao,

we sailed for Perawang near Pekanbaru, Sumatra, Indonesia.

South China Sea in the month of July was as beautifully
terrifying as ever and we even managed to catch a glimpse of the
sun a couple of days. Our old lady did a stately eight to nine
knots and the days when we reached ten, the Captain held a
party. The rolling and pitching sent a few of the experienced old
sailors to their bunks in a nasty shade of green due to sea

sickness. The sea foamed and spat and remained white with
froth. The sky remained blue black throughout till we cleared
South China Sea.

Finally, one fine sunlit warm morning, the Pilot boarded M.V.
Pratapgad at the mouth of the Kuala Siak river. We left the sea
and entered the realm of a tropical paradise. Those days the radio
room used to be shut after entering territorial waters for secrecy
purposes. In Pratapgad, my cabin had to be accessed through the
radio room. So, the radio room was open but all radio traffic
ceased. Subratada, my senior Radio Officer and I picked our
hammocks and headed for the boat deck. No sooner had we
hooked up our hammocks, the Electrical Officer and Chief

Officer came sauntering in with theirs.

We had our hammocks tied up under the lifeboat for a bit of
shade. The sun shone as it should in a tropical paradise in the
summer months but a breeze blew down the meandering river.
The Pilot had already boarded and we were underway. The
colour of the river rapidly changed from the turquoise blue of the
river mouth to the colour of a rich Calypso rum. The river
narrowed fast and soon was just the width of our ship with a few
meters to spare. The river bank was covered in thick forest and
glowed iridescent in the sunlight. The trees were so close that one
could throw a stone and it would land in the forest. ‘I'he slight

It was almost late in the mid-day when we rounded a bend in
the river and further upstream lay another small village. This
village had a small bathing pier which led into the water. From
our position the village lay simmering in the lazy heat, nestled
among the trees, bereft of any signs of life except... except that
somebody was taking a bath. From the figure at a distance it
looked like a lady in an extremely short flaming red piece of cloth
which barely covered anything. Now, we had been sailing a long
time and hardly seen a member of the fairer sex except in the
only on-board video. Within seconds word spread like wild fire
and the lower deck below us thronged with the crew. Our deck
remained relatively less populated except for the addition of the

3rd Engineer. Above us was the wheelhouse or bridge where only

We approached the village and all hell broke loose. From below
deck the cat calls and wolf whistles could rival any side alley of
Southampton harbour in the early days of steam navigation. It
grew rapidly in volume as we neared the pier but the lady kept on
bathing. Our Chief Mate, Sanjay Nighojkar and Electrical

too tried his non-existent whistling skills. Subratada and I
refrained since we were more on family terms and I was the

Now, the Pilot was an old wizened man resembling a dried
walnut and was used to travelling up and down the river
regularly. The Sukhani and the Cadet was most unlikely to start
whistling at bathing beauties in the presence of the Captain. That

in his advancing years. The excitement reached its peak when we
came abreast the pier and then the mermaid turned around

The sun started setting. We stopped the engine, anchored in the
middle of the river and settled down for the night. The sky
almost blew up with colours. A bunch of clouds ran helter skelter
across the tropical sky and the rum coloured river deepened in
colour. Parrots streaked across the sky like fiery arrows and soon
the starlit sky hung motionless over our ship flanked on both
sides by the dark forest. The sound of the generators deep within
the belly of the ship faded when I walked to the foc’sle and only a
deep silence pervaded.

Late next morning we reached our port. It is difficult to call a
jetty with two cranes and a warehouse, “port” but whatever the
shortcomings of Pekanbaru as a port, the surroundings made up
with room to spare. By the time the port formalities were over
and unloading operations started it was time to stop operations.
Pekanbaru had the delightful system of stopping work as soon as
the sun was about to set. Subrata da and I, not having anything to
do with unloading or loading operations, set out as the sun was
about to set. Light was fading as we left the port through the non-

existent gate and walked out onto a broken road.

A short walk brought us to the highway. “Highway” might be
too grandiose a term. It was metalled road which came up to the
river and stopped. On the other side the road continued

[Page 140]
somewhere inside the jungle. A small ferry was parked at the
riverside which took any cars or trucks that came up to the river
and wished to cross, across. It could just about fit one truck but I
never saw it being overburdened with two, In our stay of about
two weeks the ferry hardly made more than four to five trips a
day. However, being a ferry wharf as well as being adjacent to a
“port”, this place was of great importance and a small village
consisting of about thirty huts had sprung up in the middle of
the jungle. The port electrician stayed there, the crane operator
stayed there, the ferry Captain stayed there...in fact all the
population of Perawang stayed in those thirty huts. To serve the
trucks and the twenty odd cars which came down to cross the
river, there were four small shops which served as the local bar,
restaurant, candy shop, in short, a miniature Indonesian village
shopping mall. Indonesia was a more permissible Islamic
country and having a beer or two was not frowned or kicked

upon and beer was available.

The problem was, the beer was not cold. Subratada and I
entered one of the shops and were warmly greeted by the whole
family who ran the shop which included the grandmother, the
middle-aged couple, their son and the two daughters Eti and
Ellie. A bowl of fresh roasted peanuts and two cans of beer were
served. However, in that tropical heat, drinking warm beer was
not the best of refreshments. There was no electricity in the
village and the huts and the shop were lit by storm lanterns.

Hence, getting a cold beer was out of question...or so we thought.
As I cringed with the first gulp of the warm amber fluid, a big
bowl of ground ice, which was kept in a sort of thermocol box,
appeared. A handful of ice in a glass and the golden elixir on top

As it became dark, the jungle loomed around us. We were the
only customers in the shop. Fireflies started their dance as the
storm lanterns were lit. The cranes of our ship in the distance fell
silent as the workers wrapped up for the day. The lanterns in the
huts of the village were lit and the liquid yellow light filtered
through the tree trunks. The proprietors of our shop started a
charcoal fire at the back of the shop. Soon we were enveloped in
a delicious aroma of Satay Kambings and Satay Ayams, the
wonderful kebabs typical of Indonesia. In a short while the
workers returned from the port and like shadows disappeared in
their huts. Children laughed; voices of families mingled with
those of the myriads of crickets. The lanterns, which were hung
outside the shops, soon attracted moths and the fiery orbs of the
lantern flames soon resembled Kandahar surrounded by Apaches
of the US Marines.

Soon the ship’s officers and crew too trickled in. The steaming
piles of rice and satay arrived. The yellow light of the lanterns
glistened on the kebabs. They were served with a beautifully
balanced peanut sauce and an array of fresh pickles which looked
more like a pile of colours than food. Dazzling red of red chillies,
delicate green of cucumbers, fresh green and yellow herbs all vied

Slowly the families of the village gathered in the shops. It
seemed that not too many Indian ships had called at the small
jetty but conversation though slightly difficult due to the absence
of a common tongue (English is not a language which is
understood easily inside a rainforest in Indonesia), nevertheless

had a common topic as we soon found out.

It was Bollywood and Mithun, our own Bollywood hero, was at
the peak of his career. They knew most of the Bollywood films
and even the songs though the way they pronounced the Hindi
words, it was sometimes a bit tough to understand but the tunes
were spot on. When enough beer was downed and the pile of
satays diminished somewhat mainly due to the strenuous efforts
of our Electrical Officer Mangat, a couple of guitars came out
and even an improvised drum made of bamboo and plastic.
Mangat took out his handkerchief and fastened it on his forehead
in the iconic style of our Mithun dada and started to sway to the

song of “I am a disco dancer”.

As if on cue, the heavens opened up, lightning flashed and rain
poured down in buckets. The weather immediately fell by a few
degrees. The lanterns swayed in the wind reflecting the raindrops
creating a strange psychedelic effect. Mangat danced away to

glory in the middle of a rainforest, cheered on by a group of

For the next two weeks this became a ritual with the guitars,
drums, more singing, lanterns and fireflies, the looming
rainforest and the rain which arrived almost magically around
the same time. The only day the ritual was broken was the day
Subratada and I took a car belonging to our agent and went for a
trip to Pekanbaru, the nearest town where the only two things I
remember are the first pair of jeans I bought with my salary and
the fiery hot chicken wings which left us salivating and
perspiring at the same time. The other thing I remember from
those two weeks is my morning walks along the banks of the
river, the sky brightening and the sun coming up above the
treeline. The warm air, for there were no rains in the mornings,
carrying with it the fragrances of the surrounding forests, the
birds skimming above the honey coloured water, the young chap,
the Captain of the half derelict ferry, resplendent in his fiery red
or green sarong, greeting me from his deck and inviting me over

for a cup of tea and some fresh boiled eggs and bananas.
