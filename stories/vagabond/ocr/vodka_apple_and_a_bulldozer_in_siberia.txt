Vodka, apple and a bulldozer in Siberia

Vanino or Vaninsky is a port lying on the eastern seaboard of
Russia on the Sea of Okhotsk. In the month of August, it was just
about pleasant during daytime when M.V. Nirmal Bhushan

arrived alongside.

Siberia during summer is visually charming. The bay was blue
with rippling waves. The coast was verdant. We were portside
alongside. A short distance away a railway line disappeared
inside thickly forested slopes of the undulating countryside. The
city could be seen further away on the forward side. Solid and
stocky Soviet built houses adorned the gentle slopes and a square
was visible mainly because of an imposing statue of Lenin who
had escaped Glasnost and Perestroika in this corner of the

republic.

After shore passes were issued, 3rd Engineer Halder and myself
walked down the gangway and after showing our yellow coloured
passes to the heavily armed, imposing guard, who was still
wearing the Red Army insignia of the hammer and sickle, made
our way through the forested hillside to the city. It was quite a
long walk. Distance in Russia can be very deceptive as many

invaders have found to their cost

have found most Russian or ex-Soviet cities to be fascinating.

They have a certain aura of timelessness. Vanino was no

exception. The roads were smooth and straight and well shaded
by trees. The residential blocks looked as if they were built to
withstand ten Goerings and Wehrmachts. The traffic lacked the
oomph or Dubai... the glitz of Manhattan or the mystery of
Penang but had its own charm with suitably sturdy and old-
world cars moving around like portly old gentlemen who had
seen better days. The parks looked immaculately green and the
little children flitted in them like multicoloured butterflies. There
were huge stores selling almost nothing other than some severe
looking winter clothing, guitars and boots which were made for
Goliaths. The weather, as we hit the town, was crisp and beautiful

albeit a little cold, being peak summer.

Jalways love visiting local markets and Russian ones are even
more exotic. In Vanino, the local market was just opposite the
large store where hardly anything was stored. The clean road was
crowded with buyers and sellers. Neatly stacked bunches of
strawberries lay side by side with natty prunes. Ridiculously
cheap bottles of Russian variety of Champagne were being sold
by moustached gentlemen who exhaled tobacco fumes from well
used pipes clenched between nicotine stained sturdy teeth.
Plump elderly women with brightly coloured head scarves fought
diligent battles with young men over prices of even plumper
pears. The apples were almost as red as the lips of beautiful
young women with flowing golden tresses. Nice fresh boiled eggs
were for sale with multicoloured bottles of beer which were

cheaper than water. Fresh cherries were piled high and were so

cheap that I thought that they might as well pay me for buying a
few bunches.

A railway station was at the end of the roadside market and
looked like one of those Wild West stations, though more
crowded. The people crowding the platform did not seem like
passengers. There were no trains and everybody seemed to be
chatting and laughing and shouting at the same time. It was just
a nice big meeting place and there were no gates or any ticket
checkers around. Just next to the ticket counter, which was
shuttered and closed, there was a canteen. In Russia, almost
everything is large and the canteen was no exception. It could
house two cinema halls and huge windows with stained glass
with the sun filtering through. Not many people were inside
since everybody was worshipping the sun outside. We bought a
few thick slices of salami, some rough rye bread and a couple of
large bottles of beer and sat in a corner chatting. Time passed
and we failed to notice that the sunlight filtering through the

stained glass had grown considerably dim.

When we emerged from the canteen, the sky was overcast and
the wind had picked up considerably. We made our way to the
gate of the port as fast as possible. From the gate to our ship was
half an hour’s walking distance through thickly forested hillsides.
There was no traffic and we were the only hikers on the road.
The rain started with a pitter-patter but soon started to show
signs of turning into a deluge. When in Siberia, even during peak
summer, you do not want to be caught out in the rain inside a
forest, especially if you are from a country near the equator. Each

[Page 101]
drop was sharp as needle and painfully cold. Our ship was still
miles away but up the slope a small log hut caught our attention.

We clambered up the sloping road and soon stood inside the
shade of the awning. It was certainly not a palace being a roughly
hewed stack of sturdy wood held together by nails and
surrounded by huge pines and oaks which cast a deeper shadow
than the cloudy overcast sky. However, it kept us relatively dry
but now the rain had started in earnest and the awnings could
not stop the splatter entirely. Both of us herded towards the door
when loud voices and huge sounds of laughter emanated from
inside the cabin. We did not waste any time and knocked loudly
at the door.

The laughter stopped and somebody shouted in Russian
followed by another volley. Since our knowledge of Russian was
quite limited, Halder shouted back in Bengali and immediately
the door was thrown open. A short stout man in frayed jeans,
knee length boots and a heavy overcoat stood looking at us

inquisitively.

‘Indi?’, he asked when he sized us up and when we nodded in
the affirmative, we were immediately ushered in with the greatest
of bonhomie. There was a single room and an attached toilet at
the end of a very short passage. A huge log table stood in the
middle of the room which was creaking at the seams with the
weight of what looked like a tonne of bottles and glasses. The
table was surrounded by equally heavy wooden chairs and on the

chairs sat Russians... five of them excluding our genial host.

However, our host was the only one who looked anything
human.... the rest of the gang were Russian to the core. They
overflowed from every corner... the room seemed to be
overflowing with muscles and boots. There was a large French
window on the other side of the room. The darkness was
accentuated by tobacco fumes. The rain drummed on the

window pane.

Two chairs appeared from somewhere and we were asked to
take our seats. Everybody wanted to speak to us at the same time
but we restricted ourselves to monosyllables being restricted by
language. This proved to be disastrous. The gang wanted a nice
conversation and that too in Russian. To aid that two more
glasses were produced, filled to the brim with the transparent
liquid from the large bottles and presented with utmost
ceremony, before us. Being thirstier than Halder I took the first
sip in a hurry. The neat pure Russian vodka choked out all other
emotions. I have never tasted lava from Mount Etna but this was

the nearest thing I have ever tasted.

Halder was watching my emotions with interest with his glass
poised before his lips. He slowly lowered it and looked around.
So, did I when I could muster enough sense. We were being
watched by critical eyes with expectant faces. The burliest of
them all had a nice big moustache which dared me to say
something derogatory about the national drink of Russia. When
I managed a weak smile, the ice was broken and now everybody

turned to Halder. He promptly tooka sip and almost fainted but

[Page 103]
he had already managed to keep a nice ear-splitting grin before
he swooned.

Diplomatic relations were now restored with full vigour.
However, our quota of woe was not full yet. Two large and juicy
apples were placed in front of us. Neither Halder nor I were fond
of fruits but having them as snacks with neat Russian vodka was
pure torture. However, you do not argue with six and a half
Russians wearing soiled knee length leather boots. So, we bit into
the apples with due respect and the conversation now flowed. I
know it started with Indira Gandhi and everybody praised her
and so did we. The language differed but the spirit remained.
MIGs, Raj Kapoor and Pakistan along with NATO featured in
the discussion. However, whatever the point of discussion, our
drinking was kept under scrutiny and whenever there was any

slackness on our part, we were duly encouraged.

Now, Halder had a very interesting face. He was rather short
and dark with no facial hair and his eyes, in normal
circumstances, looked as if they are going to pop out. He always
wore a surprised expression because of his eyes and a lack of
properly defined eyebrows. Presently the vodka and apple
combined together and his eyes were about to shoot off like
projectiles. To cap it he started speaking a splattering of Russian
which he had picked up on the west coast when he was on liner
service to Odessa on the Black Sea coast. This effectively put the
spotlight on him and he was the cynosure of all eyes. He was
sitting a bit away from the window. I was nearer it and had kept
my glass on the sill. ‘his helped me pour about a peg out into the

backyard and I did this at profound risk to life and limb but
Halder was enjoying himself immensely by now. He spoke
expansively on Indo Soviet friendship and the 1971 war with
Pakistan when the US 7th Fleet was held at bay by the Soviets.
His Russian was proving inadequate for the task but he
effortlessly switched to English sprinkled with Bengali. Slowly the
level of vodka in his glass decreased and the splattering of

Bengali increased.

Drinks were flowing freely around the room and Russian,
English and Bengali, three languages separated by distance met
in a corner of Siberia. I took this opportunity to pour out another
peg over the window but Halder, by now was beyond care. I had
managed to empty about two pegs out through the window but
the remainder was beginning to take effect. The room and its
occupants were distinctly becoming hazy but still I kept my cool
since I was sure that at some point of time Halder was going to

lose his.

When he had finished his glass and about to give the impression
of the take-off sound of a MIG 21, he crash-landed in the middle
and slumped on his chair. Our hosts were not taken aback but
asked me the name of our ship which I fortunately still
remembered. The moustached man went out and shortly the
roar of engines and a loud clattering broke out above the
drumming of the rain. The burly man had brought a bulldozer
Halder was hand carried and I followed in a dignified, though
zigzag walk. Halder was lifted behind the driver’s seat. I could
barely make out the swirling steps and managed to reach the top.

There were hearty farewells and cheers from our hosts and I
waved in a carefree manner hitting the side of the bulldozer
bruising my hand quite severely. A short rattling drive and we
were alongside M.V. Nirmal Bhushan. Halder was in no
condition to get down. I too found difficulty in locating the
gangway though I managed to get down with the help of my
Russian Chauffer. The Bosun had spotted us from the deck and
the crew mustered promptly and Halder was carried up the
gangway. I got a bear hug from my host before he drove away. I

took my time but reached my cabin without further mishap.

Halder survived the onslaught. Over the years, I too have made
my peace with the national drink of Russia but even today I
wince unknowingly before I take a sip and yes, I have never ever

touched an apple when drinking.
