Khunti... that is what a stable is called in Dibru Saikhowa, Upper
Assam. I was on a budget and I had to shoot the Western
Hoolock Gibbons and during the same trip wanted to observe
the arrival of the migratory flocks in Maguri Beel. Balancing the
budget with a longish stay proved somewhat difficult and I asked
Sanjay, my henchman and brother in lens in Dibru Saikhowa to
look into the matter. He asked me whether I was willing to stay
in a khunti in which case the costs would be minimal. Not
knowing what a khunti was, all I asked him that whether there
would be a cover over my head which he confirmed. The tour

was on.

A sleepy early morning late in October 2013 my train chugged
into Tinsukia station where Sanjay picked me up and drove me
to his delightful little house near Guijan Ghat, the entrance to
Dibru Saikhowa National Park with the Lohit river flowing by.
After spending the day with his family, towards late afternoon we
arrived at the ghat where a wooden boat was waiting to ferry
people across. Sanjay and I hopped in with my rucksack and
camera bag and shortly the boat was huffing and puffing across
the swift flowing river. The Himalayas of Arunachal stood

against the sky with the sun going down.

The boat touched down at a sand bank and disgorged an
assortment of about 12 passengers including the two of us. The
bank stretched for about a kilometre and a half. After the bank
there was a sharp rise in the slope and a small village of
fishermen of some 30 huts stood there. Sanjay belonged to this
village. The sun had almost set and the village was surrounded by
forests on all sides except that of the river. The hens and ducks
were being herded inside their coops. The evening lanterns were
about to be lit and I was wondering where my khunti was. As we
walked to the edge of the small hamlet, there spread before us
were a few stables if they could be called stables. They were
flimsy grass walled structures with polythene as ceilings. The
grass wall was woven between bamboo work and somehow the
structures stood. Here I came to know that this is what a khunti
was. Sanjay was looking for Manoj’s khunti and we made our

way to one of the last stables.

Manoj, a middle-aged herdsman from Bihar, settled for a few
generations amidst the tea gardens and forests of upper Assam
had lost most of his Bihari roots. A darkish man, sinewy and
wearing an infectious grin met us at the “door” of his khunti
where he was corralling his cows for the night. The “door”
consisted of a few thin bamboos cut vertically in half and held
together by 3 sticks. The latch was a piece of string. Inside, on the
sandy floor were two wooden planks on either side. This was the
sleeping quarter, one for Manoj and one for me. I was carrying
Sanjay’s sleeping bag. I dumped the sleeping bag and my

rucksack on the plank. 'I'here was a pillow too. ‘I'he camera bag

Sanjay departed. Manoj lit a storm lantern which fought a losing
battle with the shadow inside the hut. The cows shuffled,
separated from us by a dried grass curtain. There was no bad
smell since the floor was actually sand brought in by the annual
flood of the river. The attached space behind our sleeping
quarters had a hole in the ground where a pair of chopped logs
kept a fire going constantly. Manoj dumped a few chipped pieces
of wood and blew on the fire and lo.... we had a roaring small
neat fire. It was growing cold outside.

Manoj milked one of the cows, put the milk in a tumbler, boiled
it on fire with some sugar and tea leaves and our chai was ready.

We sat down and Mangj instructed me on the do’s and don’ts of
stable life in the jungle. The only source of water was a hand
pump outside our residence. The toilet... well...... it was
anywhere because outside was the jungle. However, it was
preferable to walk about 50 meters to the water’s edge of the river
for the heavy-duty purging. Smaller calls of nature could be
answered anywhere nearby and the sand will leave no trace or
smell. Whatever the reason for stepping out of the grass filigree
residence, the first step without fail would be to take my torch
and shine it around to make sure that there were no elephants or
wild buffalos to bung a spanner in my plan of works. Other point
was to take a stick and thump it on the ground to scare away any

snakes lying in the grass outside. In case I wanted to answer the

call of nature early in the morning then I was to call Manoj to

I kept the glass down after finishing the hot sweet liquid. Manoj
started preparing for dinner. The smoke from the fire made the
light of the lamp go weaker in the knees. I picked up my trusted
Eveready, unfastened the string of the bamboo network which
was our door and peered out. The darkness almost blinded me
and I shone my torch around hoping to pick up the silhouette of
a tusker or the bulk of a bull wild buffalo. There were none and I

stepped out gingerly and switched off the torch.

The river flowed with a subdued gurgling sound. A half-moon
floated near the horizon. A cold draught blew in from the
mountains rippling the grass. The sweet smell of approaching
winter mixed with the wood smoke from the fire inside the hut
and permeated the forest. Somewhere an owl hooted and fell
silent. I looked up at the universe and the stars came down to
meet me. If ever I felt at one and peace with the universe, it was

at that moment just before my mobile rang.

The signal was erratic to say the least but now it was showing two
bars of signal and Sanjay was calling me. It was to inform me that
one of his goats had been taken by a leopard behind my hut and
he was asking me to stay indoors for the time being. I did not

require much persuasion.

Three days went by and I got used to my routine. Get up before
the sun comes up, walk up to the riverside, flash my torch to see

if I would be disturbed in my duties by one of the wild rowdies,
answer the call of nature and come back. By the time I got ready
with my camera and accessories, Sanjay’s brother would be at my
door as the light came up the horizon. Cross the village, trek
across the river bank for 1.5 km, catch a rickety boat and cross
over to Guijan Ghat. A breakfast of puffed rice or bread omelette
and pillion ride on Sanjay’s motorbike to Maguri Beel to watch
the migration. A whole day on a dugout floating amidst the water
lilies, walking through the mud and grass, getting roasted in the
midday sun and thoroughly enjoying myself. Evening time the
actions would reverse and by the time I reached my million-star
accommodation, I would be dead beat but not dead enough to
look up and touch those stars while sipping my evening chai.

On the 4th afternoon, the weather decided to intervene. I, as a
former sailor, am used to seeing and facing storms but that
evening, the clouds piled up above our heads in heaps I had
never witnessed before even in winter Atlantic or the Bering
Straits. By four o clock the deluge started. Asterix’s village could
have been pardoned if they thought that the sky was falling on
their heads. I sat inside my water proof sleeping bag waiting for
the wind to blow that crazy structure off to the Himalayas.
Thunders broke more frequently than Captain Haddock’s curses.
A hen landed on my feet and flatly refused to budge. Manoj
soothed the frayed nerves of the cattle finding his way with the
storm lantern. One part of the ceiling gave way around eight in
the evening but that was on the side of the cows. The cows were

re-tethered but the rest of ceiling miraculously held. | was half

That deluge lasted full four days without relenting. Fourth
morning the sun rose through the clouds. I fastened camera gear
to my back and made my way to the sand bank but the sand bank
was nowhere to be seen. A furious river roared and smashed the
sandy shores. The sand bank lay under a fathom of thundering
waves. There was a single dugout and the boatman agreed to
carry me with another 4 passengers to Guijan Ghat. The batteries
of my camera and mobile had to get charged and I had to get
across to the nearest electricity point available which was at a
grocer’s shop in Guijan Ghat on the other side. I clambered in
and the boat and we started. By the time we reached the middle
of the river the water was rising inside the boat at a furious pace.
It was a matter of touch and go whether we would reach the
shore first or the boat will go underwater. The oarsman gave it all
he had and when I touched down at Guijan Ghat the boat

resembled a German U Boat diving under the Atlantic.

Dibru Saikhowa is a mesmerizing place. The migratory flocks
had begun touching down at Maguri Beel where the water lily
opened their petals to welcome them. The smell of water and
grass and mud clung to me like a lover. The Wild Buffalos posed
a problem at certain times especially the time when I went to the
river early one morning to answer natures call. My torch did not
pick up any trace of any buffalo and I squatted down peacefully
when a snorting sound came from the tall grasses just behind me
up a steep sandy incline. | flashed my torch and picked up a bull

buffalo who was descending the sandy bank. I did not wait to

The food was from the jungle... yams dug from the forest...
elephant apples lying under the trees... rice and plenty of milk.
The accommodation was million-star. The devotion and zeal
with which Sanjay worked for conservation and his knowledge of
the land was something to learn. Manoj and Sanjay’s brother
Shankar made me feel that this is where I belonged and probably,
I did. The Gibbons co-operated though the rain gods did not.
Still... stars touching your head.... the wood smoke.... whispering
breeze... lightning searing the sky.... the flowing river... the call
of the Gibbons... the wings of the migratory Gadwalls splintering
the sunlight... freedom is a feeling heard by many, felt by few.

I am fortunate.
