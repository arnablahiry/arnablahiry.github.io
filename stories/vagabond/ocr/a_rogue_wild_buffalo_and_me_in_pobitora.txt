A rogue wild buffalo and me in Pobitora

I was hanging on for dear life to a branch in a tree some 10 feet
off the ground. My companion Binod was also hanging on to the
same branch. The forest guard was hiding in a hole on the
ground and a cold and calculating wild buffalo was staring at us
from an uncomfortably close range no doubt wanting to get a
closer acquaintance. That buffalo had killed two people over the
last few days and was definitely trying to get his tally higher. At
that critical juncture my call phone rang. I fished it out from my

pocket and answered.

“Dada... did I leave my jacket with you last time?”, my friend

Amit from Bombay enquired from across the ether.

Since I was not sure whether I am going to live or die on that
branch I was a bit sharp with my answer but later when I told

Amit about the incident, he pardoned me.

The migratory flocks had gathered in their thousands over the
wetlands of Pobitora Wildlife Sanctuary where the concentration
of rhinos is the highest in the world. They arrived in their
thousand to spend the winter... Ruddy Shelducks, Northern
Pintails, Garganeys, Pochards, Lesser Whistling Ducks, Northern
Lapwings all had congregated on this small patch of forest land. I
had brought my wife and two kids to witness the spectacle.

However, that particular afternoon I had to go in myself to

photograph the flocks. Binod and myself were accompanied by a

As we stood on the forest track, on our left was a large patch of
grassland beyond which lay the prize.... wetland teeming with
birds. On the right were short trees and slightly undulating land.
We left the road and plunged into grass which was almost our
height. The grass hid the terrain which looked deceptively plain.
Once inside it was tough going since the earth was dotted with
pits created by rhinos and wild buffaloes during the wet season
and then hardened into bone breakers if got your feet in the
wrong position.

As we were plunging in, the Forest Guard said “Saab... today we
have to be a little careful... a wild buffalo has killed two people

and might come this way.”

I could hear the sound of the whirring wings of the birds anda
mere mad buffalo was not going to hold me back now since I was
quite close to the wetlands and was not detected yet. I signalled
Binod to sink low inside the grass and while sinking in myself, I
told the guard, “you keep a look out and let me know if the

buffalo comes anywhere near us”.

The guard stood there scanning the grassland while Binod and
myself with my camera, crawled on all fours till my head just
about cleared the grass bordering the water. The birds had not

noticed me and were all over the place. I just waited for them to

It was December and the grass and ground were cold. The water
gave out a smell which is typically that of winter. The cold wind
created small ripples in the water as it caressed the grass fronds.
In front of me was playing out a kaleidoscope of colours... the
dazzling whites of the Bar Headed Geese... flaming oranges of the
Ruddy Shelducks.... the reds of the Whistling Ducks... the splash
on the water by Northern Pintails.... the shining sheen on the
feathers of the whirling Northern Lapwings.... and it took me a
while to shake off my stupor and start clicking.

Thad hardly focussed on the darters sitting on a mound near me
when the guard whispered in an urgent voice, “Saab.... come

back... the buffalo is coming this way”.

Getting up from a prone position, with my heavy lens in the
thick grass and soft ground bordering the wetland was no mean
feat but I managed that and took my first step to running like the
wind but put my front foot heavily in one of those blasted rhino
pits and landed flatly on my face but managed to save my
camera. Binod picked me up and we started running towards the

tree line across the forest path.

While running I tried to spot the buffalo but could not see it and
so I asked the guard about its whereabouts. The guard told me
that he could not see it either but had seen the forest department
elephants driving the buffalo inside the grass and had seen the

grass moving. We reached the path without any more mishaps
and I stopped to catch my breath. Before I could spot the buffalo
Binod urgently whispered, “Dada... climb up that tree, the buffalo
is heading this way”.

I looked at Binod and the tree he was pointing at, incredulously. I
was an expert climber but that was when I weighed about 20
kilos less. Trying to climb the tree with my present weight would
be committing suicide. “Binod.... if you are expecting me to
climb that tree, forget it... find some other way”, saying so I
turned around just in time to see the buffalo emerging from the
grass and coming straight towards us. I could have beaten
Edmund Hillary at climbing at that moment. It was true that
Binod hauled me up and the guard gave me a hefty push on my
backside but in a second or two I was standing on a branch and
holding onto another with Binod by my side, ten feet above the
ground. The guard did not get enough time to climb and I had
thrust my lens in his hands. He found a hole in the ground and

took refuge inside it.

The buffalo crossed the forest path, entered the treeline to our
left and was almost passing when Binod whispered in my ear,
“Dada, I think it has not noticed us”. As if the buffalo heard
Binod, it stopped in its tracks about 15 feet away, turned around,

looked at us and stood his ground.

There the matter rested. The buffalo refused to budge and we
had no intention of budging either. ‘I'he buffalo lowered its

massive horns and stomped its feet in mock charge from time to

time just to remind us that the tree was our safest bet. However,
even though we were up on the tree, there was one very real fear.
Just behind the tree on the opposite side of the buffalo, the
ground was elevated and the height difference was only about 7
feet. If the buffalo found that out and decided to come around
then a toss of its head would be sufficient to bring me and Binod
down. We had no hope of climbing higher since there were no
other branches nearby and the next branch was a vertical climb

without any grip anywhere.

Forty-five minutes passed and the stalemate continued but my
arms and legs now started to protest by going numb. Clinging on
to a branch with my weight and standing on another for so long
does not improve your blood circulation. In desperation I let out
a hoarse whisper to the Guard to do something. Next minute he
poked his head out of the hole and with flailing arms started to
sing, “Aja aja... main hoon tera deewana”, in a voice which would
have raised the dead. The buffalo baulked for a moment but then
lowered his head, gave out a loud snort and advanced a few feet.
The Guard promptly disappeared back inside the hole. That was
the time when network returned for a few seconds and Amit

called me.

An hour passed and the situation started getting desperate. The
Guard finally decided to go to the extreme and pulled up his gun,
aimed somewhere just above the buffalo and pressed the trigger.
Nothing happened except for a loud thud which again enraged
the buffalo. The bullet was a dud. The buffalo shook its head,
snorted but gazed steadily towards Binod and me. 'l'aking a

desperate measure, the Guard started crawling out of the hole
towards the road. Binod and myself shouted at the buffalo to
divert its attention towards us. It was successfully and by the time
the buffalo spotted the guard, he was sprinting for his life and out
of range. He ran towards the forest outpost which was not very

far away.

With the departure of the guard we were left without the benefit
of the gun... even though the bullet was a dud one. The staring
game started again. Shortly a lot of drumming came to our ears.
The guard had called the rest of the forest guards and they were
approaching us with beating drums and loud shouting. The
buffalo looked at their direction once and then returned at
gazing at us. I doubt whether even Majnu had looked at Laila

with that amount of devotion.

When the group got sufficiently close, the guards started
throwing loud fire crackers one of which landed near the buffalo
and burst with a loud bang. The stubborn chap gave the smoke
from the cracker a look of disdain and resumed his longing gaze.
Our forest Guard had now procured a new bullet now. He
walked up and let loose a shot from close quarters just above the
buffalo’s shoulders. We waited to see the sudden turn of tail and
galloping hooves of the one-ton muscle and horns as it ran away

from the bang of the gun.

All we got to see was one more look of disdain towards the
shooter and the eyes again got attached to Binod and me. Now it

was more than one and a half hours and fatigue was taking over.

My hands were slipping and I knew what awaited me if I slipped.
Those horns meant deadly business but I was at the point of not
caring much anymore.... just thought about my wife and kids
who were not aware of what was going on and the fingers found
a little more strength... just long enough to hear the roar of the

Gypsy engine roaring and coming nearer.

The news had reached Nripen and he roared down with his
Gypsy fully revving up the engines. At that sound the buffalo
took its first step back. Nripen hurtled his 4x4 through the trees
and stopped under our branch with the engine going full throttle.
Our tormentor retreated one step at a time faced with the sound
of the engine and the horn. Binod and I jumped straight into the
Gypsy from our branch and we beat our strategic retreat with the
buffalo watching from a distance with wistful eyes. I am not sure

but I think I saw the hint of regret in those eyes.

The buffalo was shot dead in a few days but not before he had
killed one more villager. I got to know afterwards that he had
been driven from his herd by the dominant bull and probably
turned towards humans to vent his anger. Nature had taken her
toll.

Pobitora Wildlife Sanctuary, a short distance from Guwahati,
hosts the largest density of wild rhinos on the planet. Hemmed in
from all sides by villages this small sanctuary is a miracle in the

sense that it not only exists but thrives in spite of all adversities.
