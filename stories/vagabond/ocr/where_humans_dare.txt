The striped form stretched itself a grassy knoll. The great banyan
tree under which she lay panting spread a soothing blanket of
shadow in the summer heat. Towards the right, the grassy knoll
disappeared into the thicket of Hental, Baain and Goran trees.
The mangroves shot out their roots (pneumatophores) into the
air in a desperate bid to breathe through the mud and saline
water. The mud-covered bank disappearing into the Chora Gazi
creek, resembled a deadly pin cushion. A crocodile surfaced
briefly from the muddy waters, felt the heat and dived back into
its cold domain. The breeze had stopped. The jungle was quiet.
Only a Greater Flameback Wood pecker kept up its drumming
on a nearby tree trunk accentuating the stillness. The creeks
meandering into the heart of the forest showed the steep mud
banks since the water had receded with the low tide. The fronds
of the mangrove covered the creeks like a mother cradling her
baby.

She lolled her tongue and panted. Her last hunt had ended in a
failure. Prey was getting scarce. The dense mangrove hindered
her charge and the herd of Chital Deer had no problems in
eluding her ambush. She had not eaten for a few days and was
hungry. Moreover, the midday sun shone mercilessly. The wind
blowing in from the Bay of Bengal had stopped. Perhaps a storm
was brewing. ‘Ihe place was surrounded by water on all sides but
the water is saline. There was no drinking water except for the

few sweet water ponds dug in the forest. Her throat felt parched.
All her prey were probably miles away and she will have to ford

quite a few rivers and creeks to have her next meal. She felt dizzy.

Moreover, the mating season had ended and little paws were
kicking inside her womb. She was young and could not
understand what it was all about except that her discomfort
increased. Earlier she could have crossed a few rivers and picked
up a nice plump cow from one of the fields but the two-legged
animals had put up a fence and her route was hampered. Only a
drink of cold water could alleviate her pain and distress before
she could start out on her quest for prey. The nearest sweet water
was about a few miles upstream along the Chora Gazi creek. She
would have to cross a few mud flats and channels but that was
the only option. She left the shade of the banyan tree and swung

towards the Chora Gazi water hole.

Young Aminul Haq was at his wits end. His young wife had
given birth to a bonny lass and he was very proud of her.
However, as the young one grew, she showed signs of epilepsy
and the nearest doctor in Goshaba town about 15 km away by
boat, had promised to heal her. The medicines were costly. His
small patch of land hardly bore any crops. The super typhoon
Aayla had breached the mud embankment and salt water had
entered his small paddy field. The land now lay fallow and barren
like a widow. A small pond had fish just to sustain him, his wife
and widow mother. They were honey collectors for generations.

His father had entered the forest to collect honey when he was a

137

[Page 129]
kid and never came back. His mother never let him venture into

the forest since then. However, these were desperate times.

The village of Jamespur had a few huts of honey collectors.
Aminul knew that they were going into the forest for the last
honey collection drive of the season. When this collection was

over there would not be another one for a few months.

He went to Jibon Haldar, the head of the honey collection team
and asked his permission to join the group. The old stringy man
with a flowing white beard wearing a loincloth knew Aminul's
father well. He was apprehensive about taking the young man
back into the place from which his father never returned.
However, Aminul's daughter's plight moved the old man and
finally he relented and gave his approval.

Aminul bade farewell to his small family. Apprehension jostled
with expectancy and fear. His father had not returned before
from a honey collection trip. One good haul of honey and he
could find medicines for his daughter. The dark alleyways made
by the creeks and the overhanging mangrove always fascinated
and frightened him at the same time. He did not know what to
think. His young wife was from a village which was at a distance
from the Sunderbans and did know much about the forest. Only
his mother, who grew up among the mud and typhoons... the
striped terror that stalked the land... who lost her husband and
brought up her lad in the grinding poverty... knew.

They started early in the morning. The fronds of the coconut
trees and the arms of the small families swayed and bid adieu to
the small expedition team. Three small wooden boats were pulled
by paddles.

The boats were vintage and so were most of the men. Aminul
looked around in wonder. He had seen the forest since

childhood. Today he was going to join the ranks of men.

This was what his forefathers had done. When the Dutch came
and set up their slave trading centres in the forest, one of
Aminul's forefathers had pursued a bee into the thickets in
pursuit of the golden harvest. When Robert Clive sailed up these
waters with the fleet of the East India Company, his forefathers
had watched in amazement from these boats. His father had seen
the last British vessel sail down these waters as the sun set on the
Empire. Aminul today would join their ranks. He knew that they
were watching him and certainly were in approval. The rickety
looking but amazingly sturdy boats were self-contained. Cans of
water were stored in the stern. A small kerosene stove stood in all
its ancient glory and cans of kerosene both for smoking out the
bees and cooking the next meal were lashed to one side. Empty

cans were ready to be filled up with the golden liquid.

The temple of Bonobibi and Dakshin Ray lay on a high
embankment on the way. It was fenced off by nylon ropes from
the forest. Bonobibi was the goddess of the forest. Dakshin Ray
represented the terror of the stripes. She had decreed that

anybody entering her domain without weapons would be under

[Page 131]
her protection. Dakshin Ray would not be able to harm them.
The woodcutters entered with blunt axes to obey her decree. She

had protected them, sometimes.

As the group sat in silent prayer in front of the thatched, broken
down makeshift temple and lighted their perfume sticks, Aminul
looked around at the grim faces of the men. Jibon Haider sat in
supplication, his eyes closed. Binoy, Mohon, Tarak, Shamsul all
were deep in meditation. Even the normally reticent Madan
Sardar, the man who they said, could sniff out Dakshin Ray, sat
in a trance. The Hindus and Muslims prayed together in a corner
of 21st century India, united by a common prayer; a prayer to
spare their lives. When the prayers were said and perfume sticks
lit, the boats set out once again. Their bows pointed towards the

creek adjacent to Chora Gazi where the honey bees were spotted.

She crossed the creeks and climbed up and down the mud banks.
The pin sharp aerial shoots made her progress difficult. So, she
left the bank and climbed into the relatively drier part of the
forest. By the time the Chora Cob pond came into view, her
throat felt like bursting. Hunger and fatigue made her sleepy. She
managed to reach the pond. Where was the pond? A familiar net
of ropes met her on the bank of the pond. Unknown to her, those
blighted two legged animals had put up the fence here in the
middle of her territory (unknown to her it was done by the forest
department to clean the pond). She could see the cool sweet

water shaded by trees. She could almost smell the fresh water.

Her body and the smaller ones in her womb yearned for the life-
giving fluid but those infernal ropes would not let her near the
water. She started circling the net looking for a chink. There were
none. She tried to bite through but those massive canines were
useless against man's invention. The nylon strands of the rope

kept slipping in between her teeth.

Fatigue overcame her. She plodded towards the narrow creek
and flopped down on the mud in the shade of some Gewa trees.
She closed her eyes and started dreaming about clouds and rain
when the drops from heaven would crash down on the forest and
she would just stick out her rough tongue and all her troubles

would be over.

Dakshin Ray probably heard her dreams. The southern corner of
the sky became slightly darker.

Aminul and his team reached Chora Ghazi creek in the
afternoon. The three boats turned into the narrow creek and
made a landing in the slimy mud. The boats were tied to the
bank. The men disembarked with their implements for collecting
honey - dry cloth, jerrycans, sticks, kerosene. Only Aminul was
asked to stay back. Somebody had to watch over the boat and
cook a meal. The boat would be much safer for him anyway

because of his inexperience.

Aminul sat back. As the men disappeared, he knew his time
would also come. When they were all gone, he sat up inside the
boat and looked around. ‘Ihe sky had grown quite dark. Rain

clouds floated in from the sea and almost touched the trees. The
muddy gullies looked darker and muddier than ever. Not a leaf
stirred. The heat simmered and rose in vapours from the slime.
The salt smell of the creek water mixed with the dank smell of

The minutes ticked by and the young man felt drowsy as if drunk
with the heat. He leaned against the side of the boat and closed
his eyes. As the salty sweat trickled down his forehead, his
thoughts drifted to the little girl he had left back home. She was

coming running towards him, her tresses flowing in the wind.

The wind had picked up. All of a sudden, her locks went flying as
the wind howled. The coconut tree in front of his house crashed
with a large crack. He jerked awake from his slumber and looked
up to the storm as it hit the top of the trees above the boat and
made a sound like the death cry of a thousand witches. Then the
massive paws and jaws severed the dreamy head. Aminul had

joined the ranks of his ancestors who had entered the forest.

My guide Tarun finished his tale, stretched and looked at his
watch. It was past midnight. Sanjeet - my photographer friend -
and I had finished our dinner and sat listening to the tales as told
by Tarun. Our boat had anchored in the middle of the river a
little down the river from Sajnekhali forest office after criss-
crossing the myriad waterways from six in the morning. The
forest was about 200 meters away, dark and menacing. The small
solar lights in the village in the distance did nothing to disperse

the darkness. Starlight and a quarter

moon had lit the night sky and the waves rose and fell in small
ripples dark enough to make us hallucinate of heads of the
striped terror swimming stealthily at our boat so much so that
Sanjeet repeatedly flashed his heavy-duty flashlight to pick out
the glaring eyes. Only the sound of the ripples against the boat
replied back.

Man-eating Tigers... giant crocodiles... danger on land and

danger in the water... honey collectors and fishermen risking

their lives everyday... sticky, smelly and salty mud... reeking
vegetation... intense stupor inducing humid heat... inaccessible
forests penetrated by narrow and dangerous waterways... and as

wild a place as you can imagine.

That’s Sunderbans.
