Pirates of the Caribbean and Yanni Yanankis

Before proceeding with the pirates, an introduction to Yanni is
necessary since he is so intrinsically involved with the climax of

the show.

Thad joined M.V. Evpo Ageos in Buena Ventura in Colombia.
Colombia is not exactly renowned for law and order. I had
managed to cross the Andes from Cali (the former drug Baron
Pablo Escobar’s stronghold) without getting shot or stabbed. The
beauty of Andes was always foreshadowed by the feeling that
something might happen. Here Andes lacked the stark beauty of
the Chilean high Andes. This was the land of evergreens dripping
with moss. The road wound up and down across mountains and
valleys through thick forests. The car crossed waterfalls which
were flowing over the road. Sturdy men wearing huge hats which
resembled the Mexican Sombrero in size plodded up and down
with pack mules carrying big laden sacks. Along with the hat
they all wore revolvers slung in leather holsters. My car was held
up at regular intervals by army barricades for check-up and every
time I disembarked to be groped by burly commandos, Still I

reached my vessel safely.

That evening, there was pandemonium on the vessel. The fitter,
Maruti Narayan Pakar, came running up, out of breath. He was
shortly followed by a man who resembled a gorilla minus the
hair who too was out of breath. He was huge and resembled one

of the more muscular participants in WWF. In facts he seemed
to sprout muscles from every pore. His face was clean shaven and
teeth a strange shade of greyish yellow from the nicotine of his
nonstop cigarette smoking. This was Yanni Yanankis the Greek
from Cyprus. He was wearing dark coloured clothes and hence
the blood was not noticeable till he entered the alleyway. His T-
shirt was liberally spattered. It transpired that Parkar and Yanni
were walking back to the ship after a sojourn to the city. Near the
dock a gang of five people suddenly started surrounding them.
Since Parkar was walking a few paces behind, he sensed danger
and took another road. Yanni was surrounded by the gang who
were carrying iron rods. Unknown to them, Yanni always used to
carry a Rambo knife on his person in dubious places. Before the
gang could pounce on him, his knife was out and had slashed the
belly of the man in front of him. Seeing their comrade slumping
to the ground, holding his guts, the rest of the gang vanished in

one direction and Yanni in the other.

Next day evening, I was chatting with the Chief Engineer, Mr.
Sampath Kumar. He was short genial gentleman from Madras
with a very pleasant disposition and mild manners. Yanni came
and knocked at the door requesting permission for shore leave.

Bara Saab was aghast.

‘Yanni, yesterday you probably murdered a man and today you
want to go out? Are you nuts? They may be waiting for you out
on the streets. If they catch you again what will you do?’ asked
the astonished Bara Saab.

This was Yanni... a giant, dangerous but humorous man and I
loved to fight with him verbally at considerable risk to my life
and limb. I could not help it. He had a cigarette dangling from
the corner of his mouth all the time and his silver tooth gleamed
whenever his grave mouth broke into a smile, which was rare.
His arguments with me generally ended with him rattling me to
my bone by shaking me like a glass of Martini if I was not nimble
enough to elude those hammer-like hands.

He had a childlike demeanour and used to get into ridiculous
altercations. Those were the days of trunk calls and no satellite
phones. Once in Curacao in the islands of Netherland Antilles
Yanni and I had gone ashore to make a telephone call to our
homes in the evening. We reached the telephone office and asked
the young petite lady at the counter to book our calls. My call
went through smoothly but Yanni’s number kept returning a
busy tone. On being informed that his telephone was busy Yanni
lost his temper and demanded to know in his Greek voice, why
his wife’s telephone number was busy at two in the morning
(that was presumably the time at his home in Limassol). Things
went quite out of hand when | spotted two police officers

Late in the evening M.V. Evpo Ageos reached the British
Guayan port of Georgetown which is the capital of the little
Caribbean country famed for producing some extraordinary
cricketers like Rohan Kanhai and Alvin Kalicharan. After
steaming for a while, we anchored in the middle of the river
Essequibo near the city. By the time the tedious process of initial
clearance was over and I cleared all the necessary messages for
Head Office in Athens it was almost two in the morning.

Next two hours I lay tossing on my bunk but sleep eluded me.
The air-conditioning felt suffocating and I opened the porthole
to let in some fresh air. The breeze floated lazily into my dark
cabin. It was still dark outside but the eastern horizon showed
the slight flicker of dawn. I could see the deck from my vantage
view point. The huge deck lights illuminated the cargo holds
which ran through midships. Beyond lay the dark waters of the
river and the lights of the city twinkled in the distance. There
were four cargo holds on the ship and the four cargo hatches half
a man high ran through the middle. There were gaps in the
middle of the hatches for walking from the port to starboard and
vice versa. Delon, our Deck Cadet was taking soundings in the
focs’le and I could make out his form. My eyes travelled back
towards the accommodation when I noticed five shadowy figures
near hatch number four which was nearest to our
accommodation. Since | could not make out the faces, | assumed

This is when Delon started walking back towards the
accommodation. He was on the starboard side and the shadowy
figures were on the port side. Only when Delon came parallel to
the figures that he noticed the figures and he stopped abruptly.
The figures had not noticed Delon’s arrival since the hatch cover

play some prank on the shadowy figures whom I still believed
were our crew when he made contact with something metallic

earnest. The shadowy figures turned around and noticed Delon
who was standing petrified. One of them pulled out a gun which
brought him back to his senses and galvanized into action. He
kept his head low and taking shelter behind the hatch cover
made a dash for the accommodation ladder. I shouted at the top
of my voice from my porthole. The Mate was taking over watch
from the Third Mate which probably explained why nobody
noticed the pirates in the first place. He heard the commotion
and looked out and shouted. The gun toting pirate was trying to
shoot Delon but the hatch cover came in his way and Delon
climbed the stairs in a mad rush and promptly fainted. One of
the crew was in the toilet and he rushed out clutching his pants.

On finding the pirates in front of him, he started shouting

Pandemonium died down and everybody went back to sleep. It
was past 0800 hrs when I stood next to the ladder at the spot
where Delon had fainted. Maruti Narayan Parkar, our evergreen
Fitter was standing by my side. The equatorial sun shone hot and

I was briefing him about the happenings when both of us
noticed five figures in the same spot that I had seen the pirates.
This time there was nothing shadowy about them since it was
broad daylight and we two were standing closest to them. The
Mate had noticed them too at the same time and sounded the
alarm bell. The pirates saw us and they took out nasty looking
swords, short but wickedly crooked. Fortunately, this lot did not
carry a gun. With the swords they heartily beckoned me and
Parker but we refused to join them for a tete-a-tete and stood
transfixed. Suddenly there was running sound from behind us
and before we could turn around, Maung Maung Thin, our
diminutive Ordinary Seaman shot out and charged the pirates. In
his hand he was carrying a trident which was fabricated by
Parkar before our arrival. We watched the little man
dumbfounded and the pirates also watched with astonished eyes

The ship was buzzing with activity by now. All officers and crew
available were running towards the aft of the ship where steel

emerged. He had fabricated a medieval looking heavy cast iron
rod at the tip of which he had welded half a dozen sharp iron

it with one hand clad in nothing but a flaming red underwear

He found me in his path and heaving that iron rod of his over the

The story about Yanni won’t complete without two more
incidents one in Georgetown itself and the next one pertaining to
the 1994 World Cup Football in USA.

These were the days when Merchant Navy had still not shed the
aura and become a floating MNC. Things used to be done
differently and a ship was a ship as it used to be for ages. We
were in Georgetown anchorage waiting to sail inland to Linden.
As in many other ports around the world in those days, many
girls had boarded the vessel. In fact, there were twice as many
girls on the ship as there were officers and crew and there were
catfights among the girls for getting business. I started locking
myself in my cabin at night to avoid getting pulled up in the
middle of the night by the girls soliciting business. I had the habit
of reading books late into night.

The other incident took place when the Word Cup Football was

probably still not born. There was only one way to watch the
match and that was by picking up the signal from a coastal TV
tower.

The Captain gave the order to take the vessel as near to the
