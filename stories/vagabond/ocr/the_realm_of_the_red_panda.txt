The realm of the Red Panda

Pradeep means lamp. He was my guide but from time to time he
was getting extinguished. The month of July in Eastern
Himalayas sees torrential rains but, on this trail, luck was in our
favour... no rain till then. However, thick clouds at 8000 feet
(approximately) kept pressing us from all the sides. We had left
the little village of Dhotrey of some 30 huts, behind, and climbed
at a rate which would have made a MIG proud. A narrow
mountain path wound up through the Pine and Oaks. Wild

flowers carpeted the forest floor wherever they found space.

Pradeep and I were accompanied by a small group of village
ladies who had ventured out to gather firewood but soon we left
them behind. Somewhere up in the clouds I spotted movement
to the left on top of a Fern tree. They were Brown Parrotbills a
lifer for me. Both of us left the track and descended down the
slope in hot pursuit. The birds acted coy and let us approach near
till I was about to raise my camera and then took flight to alight
on another branch a little farther away. Suffice it to say that after
half an hour’s unrelenting pursuit when we two emerged back on
the track we were cursing a little about the obstinacy of the

species. I did not get a single decent image.

We had reached the top of a knoll where an old man sat chewing
tobacco. Pradeep sat chatting by his side and I drew deeply from

a bottle of salted tea to quench my thirst. Another woodcutter

from the village passed by and waved us a happy smile, his
sunburnt Mongoloid features beaming. The weather, which was
misty till then, started to turn into clouds. The ancient Pines
started to lose their texture as the moisture laden mist cloaked

them. It was time to move into the Red Panda’s realm.

We left the narrow path and Pradeep plunged headlong into the
extremely dense thicket. The soil was soaked with moisture and
the path was barely discernible and sometimes vanished
altogether. Balancing my Camera and lens I groped for a suitable
piece of wood to keep me from falling down. After much
searching, I got one covered with moss and as slippery as a frog
ona rainy day. However, it served the purpose somewhat and we
carried on through that almost impassable terrain. Frankly
speaking I would have given up after a kilometre but Pradeep
kept on pushing me, “Saab, just a little bit more and we shall
reach the tree”. That little bit more stretched on interminably
through heavy fog, almost no visibility, dripping moss and lichen
covered ancient conifers soaking me. The weather was cold but I
sweated profusely as I looked down at the steep drop looming a
few inches from my feet as I tried to squeeze myself against the
facing rocks and vegetation on the opposite side. One false step
or a chunk of soil giving way and the drop would have been a

long one.

All nightmares have an ending and we reached the tree where the
Pandas sometimes come to rest. The cloud was dense and there
was absolutely no movement from the direction of the upper
branches where they generally stay. Pradeep climbed up the

slippery trunk to look for signs like excreta but there were none.
Our search was futile. It was time to climb down and the trek

started again.

Climbing down is generally not a difficult proposition in the
mountains as long as there is a semblance of a path. In our case
we lost our way in that grim dark forest twice. At one point there
was a tree whose bark was shredded into fragments by a
Himalayan Black Bear and it did not seem very old. Meeting a
bear in that situation would have been most unpleasant to say the
least. At another place some heavy animal had dug a hole in the
ground and Pradeep almost disappeared inside it. However, we
persevered, sometimes on all fours and reached the safety of the
road which leads from Maneybhanjan to Sandakphu.

Imagine my distress when I returned to Gautam’s homestay
where I had put up, to find the woodcutter who had smiled at us
had actually captured a video footage of a red panda with his
Nokia mobile phone. He had gone in the opposite direction
taken by us. The next few days were washed out by rain and I
returned to base empty handed but with a swear that I shall nail

the small chap next time when the clouds clear.
