Orang National Park

Ina rather roller coaster career spanning over thirty years, I have
been privy to some astonishing experiences. From watching
Killer Whales chasing seals in Bering straits to losing my way in
the deserts of Persia. ..getting chased by an irate wild buffalo up a
tree to watching a cigarette packet dance in a dilapidated forest
bungalow in the dead of the night inside the heart of the
Himalayas, I have had my fair share of experiences in a journey
called life. However, watching a charging rhino getting poked in
the nose by the barrel of a gun is definitely an experience worth

sharing.

Mid-January, Orang National Park, Assam...for some strange
reason, the Shimuls (Cotton tree) had flowered early in unison
turning swathes of the forest into a sea of red with a fluorescence
which almost hurt the eyes...morning fog took time to dissipate
and even at nine in the morning the wild tusker was hardly
visible as he shuffled in the shadows of the trees gorging on the
Elephant Apples...the Hog Deer grew fat from the early bounty
of the fleshy Shimul flowers which lay strewn all over the forest
floor like Red Carpet in Cannes and the night cold bit through
the layers of wool but the winter sun during the day folded you
like mother’s warmth. However, Orang’s claim to fame is not the
Shimuls or the Deer or the rhinos and elephants but the striped
cat. Orang holds the record for the highest density of tigers in the
world as of 2018, In an area covering approximately 80 sq. km

there are said to be more than 35 tigers. Just on a whim, in the
month of December, from a grassy knoll overlooking a water-
body surrounded by grasslands in the middle of noon, I had
imitated the call of a tiger and was astonished to be get a reply
immediately. My call was replied about six to seven times before
I fell silent.

Mid-January, Karel and Carina my old friends from Holland and
myself checked into Assam Tourism’s Proshanti Lodge, the only
staying option at present in Orang. Nothing much can be said
about the lodge except that it is separated from the forest by a
high wall and the food is delightful. Another private lodge is
coming up shortly which probably is a good thing. We had
planned to spend the last night in the Satshimuli Forest
Bungalow belonging to the forest department on the last night of
our stay but within a couple of hours of our check in, my local
friend Salam da announced that the bungalow was available only
that night. Some high-ranking officials were supposed to visit the

next day onwards and Satshimuli would be out of bounds.

The sun had set and our headlight wove between the trees and
the grasslands and by the time we reached Satshimuli, the full
moon was up. To understand the night, I need to give a feel of
the place though human language is inadequate to describe a
small neat bungalow without any fencing whatsoever, situated in
the middle of a forest with the highest density of tigers in the
world. The back of the bungalow has a small grassy knoll and the
land drops sharply to a half dry wetland and the grassland down
below stretches away to the horizon. ‘The front of the bungalow

as well as the grassy knoll is surrounded by Shimul trees which

That night was a full moon. We sat in comfortable chairs on the
knoll sipping hot milk tea prepared over a wood fire. The full
moon shed an ethereal light over the vast grasslands in front of
us. Huge fruit bats fought over eating rights in the Shimul trees
overhead. The cold crept up from the wetland below us and
tugged at our heavy woollens. A rhino moved silently slowly
across the grassland, a huge shadow in the silvery light. Mist rose
from the water and started forming a layer like a fine greyish
silver veil as Hog deer barked out their shrill staccato alarm calls
from all sides announcing that multiple big cats were on the
move emphasizing the fact that this forest was home to more
tigers per square km than any other place on the planet. They
were still calling when, after a beautiful hot dinner, we crept into

our warm blankets and promptly fell asleep.

Orang is a miniature Kaziranga but with a fraction of the number
of tourists which suited us fine as we had the jungle more or less
to ourselves. Khalil Chacha, my guard, was an old veteran forest
guard with an even more ancient gun (he swore that it belonged
to his grandfather from the British era) of which he was
inordinately fond. It took careful aligning to load the bullets in
line with the barrel but it looked grand and along with his
beautifully henna-ed beard and all of five feet height he posed an

impressive figure. The problem was he was scared of rhinos.

Khalil Chacha had worked in the park for the last thirty years
and spoke no other tongue than the local version of Assamese.
He was good at tracking and knew every nook and cranny of the
forest but a mere suspicion of a rhino in the bushes sent him into
a tizzy. We needed the shots but Khalil Chacha needed to put as
much distance as possible between our Gypsy and the horns. The
clash of interests was somewhat solved by me keeping one
restraining and reassuring hand on his shoulder till we managed

to at least hear our shutters, click.

We managed to pick up the fresh pug marks of tigers, a male,
and one female with her two cubs in the morning on a dusty
road and three more pug marks in the evening. However,
sighting the tiger remained elusive though we crisscrossed the
forest roads as much as we could. However, it was the beauty of
the forest that was stunning. Kaziranga, Manas, Pobitora, Dibru
Saikhowa, Tadoba, Pench etc. the places I frequent have a heavy
flow of tourists which sometimes tend to mar the experience but
Orang is a quiet backwater in spite of the incredible diversity of
flora and fauna. The only lodging at the moment is rather shabby
except for the food, which discourages tourists (the new private
lodging hopefully will change the status quo) and the forest
department is most laid back. The Gypsy drivers at the moment
are more interested in phone calls while the safari is underway (I
am trying to change that attitude. ..time will tell whether I have
succeeded or failed) and most of the other guards try to complete

the safari as soon as possible (not Khalil Chacha) and get back to

their quarters. All said and done it is truly a beautiful place with
very little disturbance from unruly and unethical tourists.

And then that fateful afternoon we were driving down a game
track with barely a couple of feet to spare on either side of the
tyres. Daud, the best driver, was at the wheels with Khalil Chacha
by his side. Carina sat on the right in the middle row and I on her
right. Karel, her husband took up the back seat. We were merrily
driving down the track and winter afternoon shone golden on
our backs. The golden hour was approaching and the green grass
had taken on a yellowish tinge. Calls of the Brain Fever bird
reverberated across the grassy glens and we were passing a huge
patch of grassy plain where the grass was very short and we could
see quite a distance except for a small clump of tall elephant grass
on the left-hand side of the track.

We came up to the clump of grass quite slowly since the track
was pockmarked with elephant, rhino and wild buffalo footprints
and as we were about to pass the clump of grass, he was standing
right by the wayside about 15 to twenty feet away. There was no
indecision, there was no hesitation. He charged. He was
positioned slightly below us since our track was a bit raised above
the field. I have never seen a battle tank attacking enemy position
but any battle tank can take a correspondence course from that
rhino. In a split second he was merely inches from me and the
mouth opened to show the huge razor-sharp teeth as he prepared
to bite me since I was nearest to him. Shamelessly I turned and
offered his teeth my backside simultaneously trying even more
shamelessly to climb onto Carina’s lap. ‘I'he teeth whisked past

right into the nose of the charging rhino. This deflected the
massive head for just those seconds that Daud needed to press
the accelerator and even over the jagged track we were gone with

the wind.

Iam quite accustomed to working with rhinos in Kaziranga and
Pobitora where I have had close encounters before but never
have I been forced to try and climb onto the lap of a lady and
offer my bum to a charging rhino before. This was a whole new
ball game and I now fully empathise with Khalil Chacha’s
sentiments - see rhino, leave rhino.
