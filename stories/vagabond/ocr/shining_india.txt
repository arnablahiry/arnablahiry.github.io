Shining India

I believe in miracles. I never did. I do now after visiting
Chandaka Wildlife Sanctuary. There is no other way of
explaining Chandaka's continuing existence as a wildlife

sanctuary.

Just about twenty kilometres from capital Bhubaneshwar, the city
is knocking at the door of the forest. The miracle part of it is that,
it is only knocking but has not broken open that door...yet. There
is a massive engineering college under construction at a shouting
distance from the entrance gate at Deras. All the agricultural land
almost right up to the gate has got signboards dangling on
bamboo poles advertising the fact that the piece of agricultural
land belongs to 'Forest View Apartments’, ‘Green Heights’,
Nature Villa Housing’ and so on. Empty bottles of Old Monk bob
up and down in the clear waters of the Deras Dam. The
waterfront in the front portion of the dam is liberally sprinkled
with discarded Lays packets and plastic lunch boxes. The calm of
the jungle is shattered by groups of young boys and girls from
adjoining engineering colleges coming in for a spot of 'fun' and
raunchy songs and yodelling ring out across the clumps of
swaying bamboo fronds. The highly understaffed forest
department staff do not have enough resources or the will to stop
the picnic. In the forest rest house, nightlong parties are held
‘The whole of the sanctuary is hemmed in between four large
cities, Bhubaneshwar, Cuttack, Khurda and Banke and the only

escape route is sealed by the mighty Mahanadi river. Yet this
could not stop half the elephant population of Chandaka to
escape to the more bountiful Atgarh Sanctuary from this slowly
tightening noose of civilization. At night, from the forest rest

house, the horizon glows red from the lights of the city.

Yet... and yet, when the sun sets in a burst of red and darkness
envelops the denizens of the forest in a veil of comfort and hides
them from the unseeing eyes of man, a slight scraping sound
comes from the undergrowth in front of the forest bungalow. A
little while later the small Kotra (Mouse Deer) voices its timid
call from the banks of the dam. The dead leaves of the Sal trees
littering the forest floor is trampled upon by elephants and the
Chital stag's mating call pierces the veil. In the tribal villages
dotted inside the park, the dark faces of the old men flicker in the
communal fire as they tell stories to the little children of the days
when India's national animal roamed these hills and valleys
before being shot to extinction by Indians. The first rays of the
morning sun are still welcomed by the calls of the India's

national bird. We have failed to exterminate them. Yet.

Chandaka Dampara Wildlife Sanctuary covers an area of 192
square kilometres. Bamboo and sal trees are most abundant.
There are quite a few tribal huts within the sanctuary. Though
half the elephant population has migrated, the other half
remains, along with the Chital Deer, Mouse Deer, Barking Deer,
Hyena, Jackal, Wild Boars, Common Langur, Rhesus Macaques
and even a few leopards - though the latter have rarely been
spotted. ‘Ihere was one crocodile in the Ambilo water hole but it

has not been spotted of late and therefore it can be inferred that

a lovely feel.

And yet... amidst this bounty of nature, the oppressive thought
keeps recurring in my mind... how long...? Just how long...? Five
years... six...? It doesn't matter. It will not be very long before
these Engineering Colleges will produce brilliant engineers who
will no doubt build the future of India. Those lovely apartments
at the fringe of the sanctuary will rise tall above the bamboo
fronds and the tribal huts and there will be beautiful people in
beautiful SUVs parked in those garages. Nice parties will be held
in the lake fronts where the apartment owners will drink scotch
as a toast to the prosperity of the nation. Some of them will
clandestinely ask a passing tribal whether a nice piece of Spotted
Deer leg might be procured at a price — ‘just for the taste you
know...’ India will be shining with GDP.

Among all this shining and luxury, somewhere in the forest floor,

the dust will be blown by the wind and settle down again but

jumbo's footprints will no longer be imprinted. The sal leaves

will dry up in winter and be carried on the breeze but the

elephants crunching footsteps on them will no longer be heard.

‘The Chital and the Barking Deer will still voice their alarm calls

for some more time till they are also gone but the trumpeting of
the herd will fall silent. The bamboo fronds will still sway and
whisper to each other when the wind gusts in spring, laden with
fragrance but those large ears will not flap to listen to the
whispers... Will the whispers turn into tears? The peacocks will
still display his tail as the storm clouds gather over the red earth
promising to turn the forest into a sea of green but jumbo won't
be there to delight in those showers. And then one day the

peacock will display his feathers no more.

My India will be shining but at what cost, and for how long? If
you love what India has stood for, for thousands of years, a deep
respect of nature and its inhabitants before being swamped by
Malls and Bollywood, visit Chandaka before we manage to turn
it into a neighbourhood park with crows instead of peacocks and

rats instead of the mouse deer.
